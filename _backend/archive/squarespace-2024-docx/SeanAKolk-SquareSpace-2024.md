Sanivation:

In 2012, Andrew, Emily, Chris, Nick and myself worked together to form a company that was focused on solar ovens as a means of heating and dehydrating human fecal maters to render it inert. We were accepted into the incubator program Start-Up Chile in Santiago de Chile for 6 months.

During that period we researched the water and sanitation sector in equatorial regions, developing countries , and developed countries. We visited refugee housing and slums called campiamentos where Chileans constructed their own houses and toilets learning more about our offering and Chilean sanitation issues. We worked with Un Techo para Chile and Un Techo para mi Pais to build test units, and work with both sanitation groups and users to develop operations procedures and best practices. We also tested humidity levels in canine fecal mater to see at what heat/humidity level work eggs would die off; prototyping solar oven and container design.

During this 6 month period we gained insight into the business, technology, and human interaction factors of solar sanitation. The company then continued one year later in Nivasha, Kenya where it serves over 250+ household and functions as a self sustaining company with goals to expand all over East Africa.

# How to came to think about goal setting: 

The first time I realized I could plot a course for life was 2007. I was working in a used camping gear shop just off Georgia Tech’s campus in Atlanta Georgia, where I had opened an Outside Magazine, and was reading a story on 10 people who were “”living their dream life.” I don’t remember what ‘most people’ were doing, but one that caught me was the story of a director of the IRC (the International Rescue Committee) who spent his time flying all over the world from earthquakes to floods, to civil wars; helping people get where they needed to go, and the supplies they needed to keep going.

I looked up what it took to get to his position, and remember three qualifications: must be fluent in french (and english), drive a stick shift, and have lived in a third world country for at least 5 years. I signed up for a french class, asked my roommate to teach me how to drive her stick-shift, and applied to a student group that had work opportunities in Nairobi.

One year later, in a Cafe in Nairobi; confused, and frustrated. I had worked with local business, learned from entrepreneurs and students in slums how they navigated life; fought policies with the group I was working with, and started a micro revolt by forming an ad-hoc union with my fellow interns (14 of us from 9 countries). It was hectic, chaotic, and wonderful. But my internship was almost up, and I didn’t know where to go next; or how to get there. Then a friend dropped a book in my metaphorical lap about working less, and getting more \[the four hour work week\], which sounded like a cheap trick, but that’s ok, the core of the book was helpful in it self.

One of the models in the book was derived from a self help speaker known as Zig Ziglar: Be, Do, Have. Define what you want to be, What you want to do, what you’d like to have. Then work backwards as to how to get it. Zig’s model stressed accountability, deadlines, and a systematic process (once you create a system that lets you succeed even when you fail, you’ve already won). It was novel for me, and oddly simple.

What do you want to have been, have done, and had by… the end? Start working backwards, list as many ‘things’ as you want. Some are giant (a house! An island! To be a Mom!) others are simple: I want to travel to Paris.

Here I was in Nairobi, 21, with 2000USD cash in my pocket, a credit card, a backpack, and the realization that I had seen more of Kenya via local buses and ex-pat parties than I had seen of my own state, playing it safe staying on a university campus. The short result is that I went overland via public transportation and hitch-hiking from Nairobi to Cape Town; hiking, swimming, sailing, climbing. The long lasting effect is that I started to systematize my plans. The end of your life is too far off; but planning for it gives you understanding of your heading and the steps you should take to begin getting there: it’s not helpful to have 500 items on a list. It is helpful to have 3. So I started charting. I made a list of what I want in life, what I want in 10 years - then in 5 years - then in 1 year: then broke up 1 years into 4 quarters.

Sometimes the year break down is big enough that I need it to have quarterly goals (saving to buy a car or go on a trip) sometimes they’re seasonally related (go skiing, or see the wildflower bloom) and are items on a calendar. The result is that I end up with a year out (at minimum) calendar of planning EVERY quarter; I focus on the Equinox and Solstice, but that’s up to you.. Once that calendar is made (and again, they change every quarter- you’re course is never set, but it’s necessary to plan that far out to start moving in that direction).

The most difficult part of this ‘system’ is accountability. It’s actually not hard to say ‘I’m going to sail around the world’ then not do it. It’s much harder to commit to that, and give a friend \$5000 and say ‘If I don’t have a boat by next year, keep this, if I do, then give it back (there are a few escrow systems like this, and you can make a different one, but you get the point.) others have used accountability groups, or running clubs (systems, not ‘goals’).

I will confess I’ve made HUGE goals which didn’t have enough accountability and therefore they flopped. I know when I plan a trip (I love planning sailing and climbing trips) that when I set a date, and pay someone… I’m gonna do it. I believe that’s why marathons with entry fees and a ‘date’ are so popular compared to ‘well I’m gonna just go run my own marathon’

If this doesn’t hit enough at the core, it could also help to go back to your values: I am helpful therefor I want to help x number of people, or this community. I want to see more trees in the world so I’m going to join this club and plant these trees.

Lets have at at. Start at your own resolution: if planning the next 3 months is what helps you great, if it’s the next decade, well ok.

The circle concept: I’ve always thought of the calendar as a circle, maybe it was a calendar I saw in elementary school, I’m not sure; but it’s how I’ve thought of it. With the New Year at the bottom, and the Spring and Summer at the top: like a clock. So when I plan my years it looks like that. A friend told me he extrapolated it into a helix seen from two dimensions with the years flowing into each other. A calendar helps you see how much time anyone one item takes: I work in weeks and weekends; where if the goal I have can’t be worked on once a week or weekend (lots of my goals are trips) then it’s not approachable.

Social Anchors:

Social Anchors: A social anchor is why people come together.

I began my social experiments in bringing people together in University when I realized the club I wanted to exists didn’t.

Seeing how Fraternities used large houses, parties, beer, and music to attract a social scene I wasn’t interested in, I found other groups who framed their interactions around biking, or climbing, or Spanish. I didn’t realize it at the time, but seeing what Fraternities offered was a great insight to all that many other groups didn’t. Even people come tother for a cause that dosen’t mean the group in it self is welcoming or inclusive. Some groups are intentionally exclusive, and possibly with good cause.

Hashers, a drinking club with a running problem, ensures they know who’s in and who’s out with songs, special names, and being grotesque enough to shake out those who aren’t interesting. There are tensions as to being too inclusive ending in lack of identity, and being too exclusive calcifying with no new blood.

Treading that line is difficult, creating the right set and setting for an event, the correct parameters of people who know each other and those who want to meet people, how objective oriented it the occasion, what is the organizational medium for gathering?

I had stumbled upon an outdoor group that was great meeting on weekend, but didn’t have a lot going on the rest of the week, communication channels were monitored, and the group that was meant for college students has aged into alumni, who have different wants and needs.

I started a weekly social gathering, which turned into several houses forming, a new sport group being organized, and a school bus being purchased. Mostly the secret was just showing up every week, and making sure people knew where and who was welcome.

Set intention, invite specifically, show up, and grow.

AuxBoard\
\
Working for a Biomedical tech start up in San Francisco I designed the electronic sensors and actuators to control water flow, water temperature, water direction with mechanical valves, several light inputs, and communications between systems. The project was originally scoped to be self contained (on-off) with manual inputs (a flow knob and push buttons) and was scaled to be controlled by a terminal.

The scope of this review is for the final year of the AUX board, which is a large shield for an Arduino Mega. Discussions were had several times about using industrial grade PLC or if the Arduino was suitable for the job (as to a beagle bone or a RaspberryPi), but due to it’s reliability, ease of use, and cost the company decided to continue using the Arduino.

The initial layout was designed in Eagle, then redesigned in Dip Trace, and finally in Altium (part of this was due to company funding over the project timeline).

The 1st iterations were hand populated, the later revisions were PCBA.

Key learnings:

- Test before scaling: This seems obvious, but when working with a part that is too small to test specific IO’s (BGA) use a different package before ordering a populated version of a board that could possible have an untestable trace. I also had issues with jumping too quickly into PCBA’s when I would have learned more about my components by populating them myself.

  Ensure identical parts: I had issues with a few capacitors that were not identical, and had different tolerances.

  Test points: For almost any part of the board having a test point or a jumper connection is extremely helpful in the splay out board. The two most obvious places to not do this are on Antennas and in-between a clock and the IC (ops).

  Star points: Start points are for boards that have multiple voltages on the same board. The Star point is the location where all the grounds should meet up and overlay so that there isn’t ground variance. In this board there was 3.3V, 5V, 24V, and an Isolated Analog signal. Organizing the layout so that the star point a relevant place for all of these was a major design consideration.

  Analog digital isolation: Analog and Digital signals must be isolated from each other, Grounds included.

  The Molex micro connector catalog: There are lots of little bumps and nooks for these connectors, I got to learn and place (design from scratch) many of them, along with constructing several test cables.

  LED verification: LED’s are cheep, and easy to place. If cost isn’t a huge factor (for industrial machines, as opposed to consumer electronics) or you’re making a prototype board, place them everywhere, power on, ic power, resets, basically any IO

  Factory turn around times: If a factory tells your it’s moving but your order shouldn’t be affected. Don’t believe them.

Boatless Sailing ( For those who don’t own boats)

Boats might be the best worst idea ever, however they’re the best incarnation of a metaphor that’s ever exsisted. They’re floating houses if houses could sink, rot, and run into other people houses and cost more than your house. So Being stuck in the same house with your temporary family for a week is similar to summer camp where everything that can come up does, and your can run away because you’re all sleeping less than 30 feet from each other.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image44.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.875in" alt="IMG_20180713_145402596_HDR.jpg" />

I began sailing six years ago, and immeadatly found it frustratingly amazing and one of the most rewarding group experiences I’ve ever had. After a trip for almost two weeks in the Sacramento delta I realized I needed to make way into getting my own floating disaster, and quickly found myself with a 25 project and two co-owners. We spent the next two years arguing, fighting, and paying money we didn’t have on a boat that was fun, but stretched between all of us.

Since then I’ve come to the conclusion that sailing other peoples boats is great, even if you have to rent them, because owning your own boat (unless your living on it), never seems to be worth the cost. My first charter was to Santa Catalina off the coast of L.A. for one week we sailed, dove, spear fished, hiked, and feasted. It was a great trial run, and I was hooked on the idea of going far away places, and bringing people together.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image62.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.31944in" alt="DSC00121.jpg" />

Since then I’ve sailed in Thailand, Mexico, Croatia, and the Seychelles. I’ve taken classes with American Sailing to learn more about Bare Boat cruising, and became licensed in radio operation. I’ve captained for over 50 people, and we haven’t lost anyone yet.

Sailing brings lots of different skills together. It requires lots of planning, boat knowledge, logistics, coordination, cooking, medical know-how, wild life identification, and lots of interpersonal communication. Figuring out why everyone is on the trip is crutial, ensuring everyone feels safe and heard. We have nightly crew reviews, one on one check ups, roll changes and updates for situations that aren’t working out.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image54.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.31944in" alt="DSC00129.jpg" />

Little things become big things, and unlike other activities, you literally have no where else to go. The Elephants in the room have to be addressed, and crew members have to confront each other. Due to the intense nature, friendships are formed, sometimes issues that have been underwater for a long time brought up.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image23.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:3.65278in" alt="IMG-20180711-WA0006.jpg" />

We’re almost forty people in our charter sailing group, many members have come back several times, and sailed around the world. Some have gone on to race, other to learn ro fish, cook, and even acquire new skills outside of sailing, such as knot tying and taxonomy.

If you’re interested in knowing more, send me an email , and feel free to ask. We seem to have a trip every six months or so, and the locations keep getting weirder and more out there!

# Sailing the Adriatic from Split

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image42.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.875in" alt="IMG_20180713_145402596_HDR.jpg" />

**Split, Croatia.** Kate and I Had been hiking and climbing in the Alps (Slovenia, German, Austria, and Italy) for three weeks before making our way to Croatia. We arrived the eve of Croatias World Cup game against England, and unbeknownst to us; ULTRA a city wide electronic dance music (EDM) festival which was taking place around the city and had parties on boats in Split’s harbor.

Our goal was to sail out of AMC Split Marina Saturday as early as possible and make way for one of Croatia’s several hundred islands.

**7/5: Split:** Kate and I arrive in Split, and make our way to Wonderful World Hostel in old town. We meet up with two other member of the crew: Audrey and Ned, who have been here since the night before and understand the layout of the town. We deposit belonging and set out for our first crew dinner, where we meet up with Sarah, who had just gotten in.

First crew dinners are usually about reviewing the outline of the trip; however we still had two more crew in transit; so we went over the next day, the marina, supplies, food, and a course of sail. Klemen, who hadn’t arrived yet; is a Slovenian local, who’d been to the Split area several times, free diving and spear fishing; would be helpful in our decisions later.

**7/6: Split**: Sean gets rope/checkout marina…. Christiana shows up. We split up, half going for the morning market, half investigating more general grocery stores. Getting food is an odd balance since you don’t want to store the food too long before it’s on the boat, when you don’t have anywhere to hold it.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image11.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.875in" alt="IMG_20180708_201235200_HDR.jpg" />

**7/7: Split:** We weren’t supposed to check into the marina till 4pm, which is a lot like losing an entire day of sailing; but I talked my way on to the boat at 2pm, we still didn’t get checkout out till about 4pm anyway, but we walked on the Beneatue 35 and got a sense for it’s layout and rigging. Fortunately we were able to get a car parked in the Marina, so we could start storing food and bags there around 12pm. There were probably 200+ boats of various sizes in the marina, and at least half of them were attempting to leave Saturday. However, the wind had picked up (we would learn the wind report was unless at best) and there was a world cup game at 6pm. As we rushed to load the boat, go over a quick checkout, with some not so accurate descriptions (and realizing the water tanks hadn’t been filled). We attempted to head out of my first Mediterranean moore. We hadn’t even let of both of the stern lines, before the bow started to dive leeward, and we realized it was too much to leave on our own in such tight conditions. We tried calling both the marina, and our charter company for help… but with a game on, and the sun setting… I admitted defeat, and prepared to settle in for the night at the marina.

**7/8 Split, Golden Horn,Hvar, Stari Grad, Brac:** 09:00 After a night of heavy bass drumming, and sunrise children returning to their beds (there are several yacht week flotillas that take crew out for a week of partying, who were in for the night). Klemen and I prepared the boat, received help from our next door ship to spring around (cantilever) their bow. With the slight help, we were off at about 9am, motoring out of the mouth of Slip, and into the Adriatic sea and the Dalmatian coast.

13:00:: Our original plan was to head to Brac for the the the 7th, but we had enough time now to set out sights further. With light winds out of split we motored south to the straight between Brac and Slota, to come out to moderate winds. We set sail and headed for the Golden Horn, a crowded beach, where we set anchor under sail and had crew go to shore for an hour. Then the winds picked up to 24kts, and we sailed under heavy conditions to Stari Grad, a developed town, were I undertook my first med moor (no anchor), backing in among the other expensive rentals. We had cafe and restaurant front seats. The crew dispersed, seeking wine and cheese, and met back for dinner.

**7/9: Stari Grad, Brac:: Paklinski Islands (Single Anchor.):** After a Cafe Stop in town, we motored out to light wind, with Christina at the helm, under her Ned and Sara trimming. We found wind two to three miles out, and set sail on a close haul. The wind continued to pick up as a pulled around Hvar’s west point, and we realized we didn’t have a cove or course set. We also realized we didn’t understand the terminology behind heading, course, bearing. So; we hove-to (another teaching moment!) researched our options, and what words we should be using, and made in for the Paklinski Islands. We found that we were late by Croatian flotilla standards, and all the mooring had been taken. We also realized our captain and the helm we’re communicating, so there was some distress over where we were going, boat traffic, and if we should be sailing or motoring. The setup of the SunSail 444 was such that is could be more or less single handed with possible exception of raising the main, and even the there was a powered winch. The auto pilot was dialed in so that over correction of erratic turns we not a problem, and I was never in fear of leaving the helm for a second to grab a radio. # Exploring virtual depths to understand how to play with your comfort zone

> The concept of ‘type 2 fun’ was introduced to me when I first went caving; “it’s like fun but different.” Where Type one fun is fun now, and fun later; type two is not fun now, but ‘fun’ later; it's a struggle that was worth it; it’s delayed gratification towards pursuits that you are striving towards. The concept is used in other pursuits ( I hear it mostly in outdoor sports, but I’m sure beyond as well), and gives a useful framework for understanding where that line of ‘fun’ which might better be described as ‘comfort’ and ‘not fun’ or stress both for yourself and others.
>
> Challenges are by definition not easily attained; but if they’re too challenging; too stressful, too out of our level of control, then it’s not fun; can end in suffering, and maybe long lasting trama.The Inbetween spot; just the right amount of stress (in a good way) and just out of your comfort zone is where growth lives; just outside, but not too far; close enough that you can normalize, gain comfort, understand the lay of the situation, and then grow into this space (whatever it might be).
>
> A great example I found recently is a game that mastered this progression like nothing I’ve ever dealt with; and it did it without increasing your ability to vanquish your ‘opponents.’ Subnautica is a survival game based on an aquatic alien planet that refuses to give you any useful weapons and creates situations requiring you to constantly face your fears; creating incredibly uncomfortable situations for a game that isn’t specifically horror themed.
>
> You are dropped onto an ocean planet in the literal shallows; you have your life raft as a home base, and you can see the ocean floor a few feet below; you are given little to no instruction, and have the constraints of needing food, water, and oxygen; which creates time pressure . Pushing to the edges of your shallow area and going deeper allows your to build news tools and makes it easier to sustain yourself. Then night falls, it gets dark, and you have to cope. There are a few aggressive animals in the area, but they don’t chase you far; they’re not enemies; just protective of their space; you could ‘die,’ but that’s not really the challenge of the game. Subnautica creates a comfortable, safe space to normalize.
>

The recent increase of seaweed blight along the Caribbean has forced the carbon markets to devalue the previously reliable CO2-capturing ability of the Florida shelf colonies. The Panasonic - Mondragon Co. is moving its graphite production floating factories, dependent on the kelp harvesting, northward early this year to the Hudson Bay and the North Canadian Aquatic-Green Ecosystem.

This market destabilization and the crowded coastal aquatic cultivation areas (sounding:100m up to 12miles offshore) along the entire North American continent, have provoked new off coast experiments by the Ocean Frontier Foundation in exploring the viability of the Wandering Kelp Forest Project. The floating kelp forests would contain the required infrastructure to generate kelp and other traditionally coastal based life off coast at a depth of 30-10m, so avoiding shipping traffic and weather anomalies. Standardized at 1 hectare per unit, the largest test projects have interlocked over 100 hectares to drift the Pacific Gyre for up to one year at a time with minimal interference to shipping traffic.

One of the main challenges to the Wandering Kelp Forest Project has been it’s structural longevity in maintaining neutral buoyancy at sea for while accruing biomass. The Ocean Frontier team breakthroughs in elastic polymer fiber technologies and active pressure monitoring systems similar to the cargo dirigible—now in operation over Central Asia—that use helium as ballast, have allowed the Foundation to gain the confidence of industry skeptics.

Robotic monitoring and harvesting have created new niche industries of locally engineered defensive biological units to target the mites and lionfish. These problem species can permeate the experimental Wandering Kelp Forests if not monitored and destroy the defensive outer nitrogen ‘fence’ kelp and filter mollusks. The micro and macro plastics still pose a threat to the Forests, which use a combination of biological and robotics defenses to ensure a productive and lively ecosystem of kelp, mollusks, and feeder fish. Recent Forests have recorded accounts of being productive enough bio activity to attract whale sharks and manta rays, species normally associated with coastal populations.

The Wandering Kelp Forests are expected to receive full scale implementation in the next 2-5 years with contracts pending with both Exxon Pacific Energy Company and BP-Atlantic, and increase carbon capture of 70 gigatons by 2060.

# [A letter to an aspiring mountaineer](https://www.seanakolk.com/log/2019/3/28/a-letter-to-a-friend)

A competent mountaineer knows how to lead; and going up with guides who's intention is guiding and not instructing won't get you there when teaching you to follow doesn't build leading skills.

Big mountains are sexy, but often not the places you learn how to lead. It's the repetition of skills, the building of a risk model, the communities who give you constructive feedback and push you to expand your boundaries.

A good way to start is by setting a baseline of agreed upon actions (this is known as the 'shu' state in zen) Do as your told (always use this knot) and apply it to your environment. It's important to stay within bounds when you don't understand the boundaries. This is the following a guide state; and it isn't a one time thing, it's broad; it applies for each new skill you learn; you are always going back to the beginning; each new surface to climb, each new language to speak, each new tool to play with.

After your've followed the forum for long enough and have defined the risks and uses of the skill; you realize that you want to lead, you progress into the chasm of the novice - intermediate syndrome. Where your need to push your own boundaries, and experiment with other to understand what the forms and the 'truths' mean. It's asking from instruction and then going out on your own. It's finding the boundaries yourself and suffering consequences of miss-steps that a guid would never let you take. It's building understanding of how far you want to push yourself and how you want to interact with other as your followers, your equals, and your betters. This is known as the 'Ha' State in zen; it's putting jazz into your step. It's seeing that the strict form was there for a reason, but that poetry has a purpose.

This is where community helps, where the helpers are looking for the next generation and those who've come over the commitment barriers. This is where time and energy need to be played with and nothing but effort will give you an understanding. Where you find the meaning to 'why' you're playing, 'why' you're sweating, and 'why' you're taking others with you. There are many camps, many flavors, many lovers and haters.

But find that fire that warms you. Find the camp with your right mentors; and pay them. Sometimes they need money, sometimes they need companionship, sometimes they need a catch, sometimes they just need a friend, or a student, or someone to listen to. But contribute, and learn, and give back. Help keep that fire going.

And eventually you'll make it out. You'll see that the long dark valley of the soul, the effort you put in, the obscurity, the hardship, the betrayal of trust, the frustrating arguments over... what? And it might end in you leaving - it's still out, it's still over.

But if you keep on, and keep going you'll be in the 'Ri.' The master who understood why the forms were important, those who saw why leavening them is necessary, and experimented with the different hows and whys. The 'Ri' no longer needs to follow the forms because every act is instilled with the underlying philosophy of intention. The strict limits of laws don't make sense because they understand the system, and the system of systems and the different levels we're all playing on and how and why we need to be there. This can only come from the experience of experimenting, and error, and many trials.

so once you've seen that you want to learn, you need to know the basic steps. Find a mentor who's willing to teach you so that you can then lead yourself. Find the community that allows safe experimentation; is open, and wants new members, and wants them to go places. Be interested, the hard work isn't sexy. The summit photo shot doesn't prove much. Experienced climbers have turned around more than they've summited, and have waited for days in small tents and cold huts waiting. Keep learning, talk to the old timers, and be careful of the bold. The mountains will still be there, and it's a sport of ego; we don't 'have' to do this, we choose to, so go with heart, and go for the right reasons, your reasons.

Transferring experience into learning isn’t as simple as having had that experience, there’s also the processing of those mistakes and experience; solidifying those learning’s by reflecting on the actions taken. Mastering those skills and knowledge and turning those learnings into wisdom (a term I would never use in front of anyone you’re attempting to lead or instruct) by teaching others and seeing where the methodology is in unrefined or un-transferable.

As I was paragliding in the French Alps in 2018 I ran into a British Pilot who befriended and took me under his wing while we camped in the same site just outside the town of Annecy in the paragliding capital of France; Doussard. I had just achieved the rank of novice pilot (P2) in USHPA, and was making my tour around Europe. I was getting in over my head and flying with guided groups cross country, with, what almost every pilot I met said was ‘too little experience.'

Dominic didn’t critique my actions, instead he asked what was the logic behind my decisions, or 'what precautions have you taken for this scenario.’ He was less interested in hard set rules, and more interested in the and why’s rather than what. This is difficult in paragliding, since unlike many other outdoor activities, you really can have fatal mistakes paragliding, and almost everyone knew someone who’s no longer with us. This leads to stark reactions to those not following norms, and protective behavior of launch sites. All of this is understandable, but still difficult to tread as a new comer in a foreign country.

As we talked throughout the week he brought up concepts and adages that gave me confidence, or at least perspective on the conversations I’d been having with other pilots: “A pilots can fly 100 of the same hour, or a 100 different hours, a lot of these guys have more hours than you, but have only flown two or three sites, you’ve already tripled that in a fraction the time.” I was traveling solo, but always flying with others, never taking off first, and asking as many pilots as I could what to watch for, how to handle a feature, or and if there was anything else I should know.

As a point of scene, the majority of pilots I met were older (50+) males who had been flying over ten years. Paragliding isn’t an cheep sport to get into, and much like sailing, everybody seems to have an opinion on every little, thing, and you’re always doing something wrong in someones eyes, it can be tiring navigating the social aspect even without the actual act of flying.\
In one instance, a french Pilot asserted concern over my wing, he said it was too advanced for how much time I’d been flying. Dominic, asked why he was concerned, in which he responded that he would feel bad if I was injured and he hadn’t said anything. Dominic pointed out that he had now said somehthing, and I was aware of that, and had thanked him, and that It was now up to me to make my choices in the matter.

I learned after this encounter that Dominic was a retired consultant, and he was employing a simple technique call 'Active listening.” It was almost as if he handled every encounter with 'soft hands.’ He was ask how, and why several times before ever going into a fact. It was in a way setting the stage so that anyone could give input, and informing them that once they had accomplished that, they had the choice in how to proceed next. It was a way to understand feedback, unwanted advice, and process the desired and objectives of those I was encountering.

As we continued our week flying Dominic told me about his experience consulting, and the company he had founded as a result of his motorcycle enthusiasm; training police and riders on new technology changes (paradigm shifts) and how to adapt riding styles to the development. His core belief was that if you weren’t progressing your were regressing, and the best way to continue improving was to keep stepping up the ladder, and checking off skills, even if you don’t think they’re immediately relevant. We also talked about the importance of guided experiences; how taking your comfort zone further with the help of guides allows you to normalize risk that you can undertake later without the aid of others.

All of this was solidified when just a few days later, I traveled to the Swiss alps with my friends who were practicing acro-paragliding, which I was neither set up for or prepared to do, and they gave me the opportunity of practicing collapses, spirals, surges, and partial stalls (maneuvers I was always hesitant to do without a lake, or the promise of a quick ride back up to launch), and finally my first tandem flight, where my friend took me far beyond my comfort level and showed me wing overs and really took my past my personal comfort level, while showing me what to expect and prepare for.

By experience a broad range of personalities, teaching styles, and environments I’m exploring the tools and lessons I learned by writing and reflecting about these topics in their own scope, and in in hopes that I have the time and ability to pass on what I learned to others.

# [Freezing People](https://www.seanakolk.com/log/2016/2/1/freezing-people)

This past weekend, 11 friends and myself wound our way up to Lassen Volcanic National Park, the 'Forgot National Park,' to test out the snow, and see how suitable it was for keeping us warm. This turned out to be a horrible idea. Never go here, stay far, far away. Don't even go north of Sacramento, there's nothing to see up there. That being said. woah. It was cold.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image66.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.875in" />

Four of us went up Thursday, to realized it was raining that night, and most of Friday, so we hung out around Redding, reading, and preparing till Friday Night. We arrived at the South entrance at 4pm; to... what was probably technically snow, but might be better described as falling slush. There was already a 15ft base layer of snow, so it was more of a comfort issue than a transportation thing.

Of the 6 of us there (we met Michael and David at the parking lot) 5 of us decided to try hauling sleds, which we had to put together, and test gear, in the rain/sleet/slush.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image31.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.875in" alt="Pre snow!" />

Pre snow!

This took Two hours, so we left the parking log at around 6pm, in the sleet/slush/rain darkness. It was hovering around 32\*F unfortunately, which is the worst temperature it can be.

After 2 miles, lots of group separation, and extremely sore hip flexors, the rear guard called it a night (to the dismay of the half mile ahead forward group.)

We set up camp in the dark, and crashed hard. Sleeping in the snow isn't all that different from 'normal' camping, except in sub freezing temperatures the only way to dry anything is to put it in your sleeping bag with you. This includes keeping any water you want liquid the next day in there with you. So we set up tents, bags, and stripped off our wet gear in-tent. Then took anything we wanted for the next day and brought it in our bags with us.

We slept in till 9, and some of the group left around 10. We still had 2,000 feet to gain, and at least 2 miles till our destination of Lake Helen...

The Idea for Freezing People (working title until a better one arrive for next year) was that interesting people will overcome barriers to go interesting places. Anywhere you can just 'pay' to get becomes less about the journey and what you 'can' bring, and more about how much you can spend for the experience. Events such as Burning Man (initially), Ephemeral Isle, and The Rainbow gatherings exhibit this. So rather than 'lets go build igloos and sleep in a cabin' the event became, let's travel somewhere semi-assessable, and explore how we operate without our normal comforts.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image41.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.875in" alt="Photo:&amp;nbsp;Bennett Chabot‎" />

Photo: [Bennett Chabot](https://www.facebook.com/bennett.chabot?fref=photo)‎

When journey dictates that you consider what you can bring with you, the importance of every object takes on more gravity. At Freezing people, you most valuable object is your sleeping bag, without it, you actually can't survive the night. next might be your stove, the only way to get water where all other liquid sources are frozen.

We skinned (skiing uphill), trudged, and tiptoed in the new light of the day, contrasting to the sleet of last night with sun and views of the park. The party at camp (getting a slow, well fed start) radio'd in that 8 more had joined from the parking lot, putting us at 12! The early starters arrived at the final destination by 1pm, and started to set up the entrenchment.

The ways people express themselves at an event like this are in 'how' they approach the task at hand. Some brought skis, other snow shoes, some had sleds, others went ultra-light and counted every ounce. We all brought shovels to dig snow, and pat down ares to sleep. Some were just comfortable enough to keep warm and dry, and that consumed most of their time and effort. Still other needed...a bit more, and dug out an snow cave that could hold nine people.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image18.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.875in" alt="Photo:&amp;nbsp;Bennett Chabot‎" />

Photo: [Bennett Chabot](https://www.facebook.com/bennett.chabot?fref=photo)‎

Weather was against us this time, the event was supposed to be three nights of camping, and time to explore around the lake, and possibly event summit Lassen peak. However, acknowledging that fighting weather is a futal effort, we hunkered down, and enjoyed building our cave, meeting people who for the first time were sleeping 'on' snow, and learned about how to keep ourselves warm (and alive) in frozen wilderness.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image58.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:4.875in" alt="IMG_20160130_093911.jpg" />

Night dropped temperatures down into the teens, as we heated mulled wine, whiskey, and assortments of high fat, high protein dinners. Topics around the camp stoves musing over weather or not we could feel our hands, how best to keep feet warm, and what the idea of 'fun' is.

Tents being as small as they are, having anywhere for people to sit out of the wind, and be even slightly warmed (in a snow cave!) is a blessing, and as it turns out, only one of us had ever constructed such a dwelling before.

<img src="_backend/archive/squarespace-2024-docx/media/image32.jpg" style="width:6.5in;height:8.66667in" />

The next day, as quick as it came, had us slowly packing up, to head down the 6 mile trail, skiers in lead (some even taking real turns down). It was as sunny and bright as the day before, all 12 of us observing different methods of winter travel, and styles of warmth. We traveled back without innocent, to beers and warmth of Chico's Brewery. New friendships made, new trips to be had.

Until next freeze, Thanks to all who made this possible.

-S
