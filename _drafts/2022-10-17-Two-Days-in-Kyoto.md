If Tokyo is walkable, then Kyoto is enshrined, and I wouldn't limit that to what human hands have done. In Tokyo, buildings are new, meticulous, packed in, well-thought, but mostly new. Kyoto is filled with old timbers and knotted trees, a testament to a different kind of care than the stone cathedrals of Europe: one that takes oiling, painting, gardening, and attention.

I went to Kyoto with objectives, and much like Tokyo, found those useless after a few hours of ambling. I succumbed to slowly drifting from one place where hands had spent time with trees to the next.

I found a shrine to the harvest, foxes and arches in abundance, paths wandering away from the tourist crowd, new and old houses, and talk of Airbnb. Another shrine to Buddha, and a third to something I don't know because I was busy chasing a Japanese Wagtail jumping through a creek.

The amount of social agreement that has gone into the organization of the shrines, the forests, the clean streets, the quiet alleys, the clean streams, and the level stone gutters is still incomprehensible to me. Every Japanese person I've met has been helpful, and I've had more than a few conversations with service providers via Google Translate, bemused smiles on both sides, communicating through screens but present in person.

I can't help but compare what I see here to everything I've seen before.
