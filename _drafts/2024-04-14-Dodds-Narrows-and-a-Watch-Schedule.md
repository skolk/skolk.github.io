With the prospect of adventure, the shift into pursuit, and a new crew of co-conspirators, fatigue is almost inevitable. With longer distances, the urge to push is easy to justify: "we have so much further to go." And yet the body rebounds from any push with a pull, and the crash arrives the next day.

After a long day crossing from the San Juans into the Gulf Islands, we slept until sunrise at 6 am and made breakfast underway on the glassy waters of Pender Island. A watch schedule took hold. It was fascinating to watch the different recovery strategies at work: one crewmember would go below after a watch and fix small boat problems or start a routing project; another would stay on deck and read or cook. A rotation lets a crew go significantly further. What rest means can differ, but the context change helps everyone.

I put Shakshuka on: soft-boiled eggs, red peppers, tomatoes, olive oil. The motor thrummed as we rounded Salt Spring Island up to Dodds Narrows, which had to be crossed at slack or close to it. The concept of "gates" becoming real: a current too strong to fight, only passable around slack tide when the ebb and flood change. We dropped anchor to wait, made lunch, and headed in slightly early with a 2-knot push.

Out of the Gulf Islands, just as quickly as we'd sped through the San Juans. Nanaimo is the second-largest city on Vancouver Island, and we rushed for groceries, finding fresh vegetables we couldn't carry across the border.

Dinner was quick and good. The next day called; alarms set for 0200.
