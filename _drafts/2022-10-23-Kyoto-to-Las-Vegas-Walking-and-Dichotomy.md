Kyoto, which translates to "capital city," was the capital of Japan for over 1,000 years and was mostly spared from bombing in World War II. It has over 2,000 shrines and remains the cultural center of Japan. Vegas, meaning "the fields," has been a watering hole for centuries but began to grow during the Gold Rush and was a Mormon fort between Salt Lake and Los Angeles. It gained most of its force with the construction of Hoover Dam, and a different kind of environment for personal fulfillment among the workers who wanted time off the clock and away from any protestant ethic.

For me, both locations were for conferences and took drastically different emphases on the ability to stroll and how to obtain the small daily things. I really do want to disparage Las Vegas, and yet it brings joy to those who seek it, and I found the ugliness within me in the comparison.

I often recommend the area to visitors: Red Rocks is accessible and spectacular, Zion and Canyonlands are close by car, and the desert has purples and red-oranges you don't find elsewhere. The strip is filled with people seeking joy from a roll of dice, a sip of a cup, and their friends. That's not so different from me.

I have more thoughts on robotics and space, which I'll get to later. But I'm grateful for the dichotomy and the reflection it forces. That said, I still prefer a walkable city, like the one I'm writing from now in Mexico.
