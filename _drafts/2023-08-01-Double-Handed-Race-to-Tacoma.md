This past weekend Kate and I sailed down to Tacoma from Shilshole on our second double-handed race (only two people allowed), with kites flying. This gave us time to work through kite rises, douses, and gybes. There were fewer wraps this time, and the light wind made mistakes easier to adjust for.

I'm still learning, with Kate's patience on the boat, to meet people where they are rather than becoming frustrated that they aren't able to read my mind. It's almost as if I would demand the wind to shift, or the rain to stop, or the waves to abate. Meeting people where they are and working with them on the path they're walking is an act of service, and if it isn't, that's the thing worth examining.

The most useful practice I've picked up in the past few months: take a deep breath, then take 20 seconds to exhale while relaxing every tense muscle, and think about how to let go of what I want, what I'm demanding, what I'm frustrated about, and how to meet the moment with patience. It's the opposite of demands. And there is a lot to demand on a boat when "everything is going wrong," and it's easy to blame your crew. But it's almost always the eye of the beholder, with desires for things to be different instead of attending to now.

Good lessons this weekend from my crew, my fellow sailors, the race committee, and the Sound.
