At 2am we woke up, raised anchor, and motored out. I took the first watch as the moon rose and dolphins splashed around us in the dark.

It was almost Kate's watch when a squeal started. Kate got out of bed and went to the engine. We switched places. I took the cover off and killed the switch. We were in a large channel, 2 to 3 nautical miles from land. Kate hove to as I inspected.

The alternator belt had come off. The engine was at 240 degrees; operating temp is 190 max. I sprayed everything down with water and it just evaporated. Knowing heat was the problem, I checked the impeller, which looked fine. When I restarted the engine briefly, water was exiting the raw water port. I thought maybe the alternator had jammed and snapped the belt. I had a spare for exactly this purpose. But before I installed it, I checked all the pulleys, and found one that wasn't spinning at all.

After reading the manual: internal freshwater impeller, moves the antifreeze around to cool the engine. I got the antifreeze out with a trash bag and funnel, took off the part, and found I could only twist it with great force. The bearing was off-kilter, the whole gear seizing. It was 6am.

Kate told me to come watch the sunrise and take a nap. We sailed south, back the way we had come, to get cell signal and research options.

That's why we're sailing south the long way toward La Paz, motorless. It's been a stressful day, short on sleep, and a few arguments about how to sail, who's in charge, and what we're trying to accomplish.

That evening we made our way around Isla Carmen and sailed into Puente Colorado. It was Kate's first time anchoring under sail.
