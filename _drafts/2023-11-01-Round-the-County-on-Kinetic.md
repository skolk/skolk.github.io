This past weekend Kate and I joined a 35-year-old tradition of sailing around the San Juans. We started from Anacortes on the J105 Kinetic and motored out at 6 am into 2-meter swell and headwinds crossing Rosario Strait, almost losing the lifesling and various other deck items.

The boat we had planned to sail with had sold earlier that week. With only three days to find a new ride, we put out the crew signal, and thanks to some quick work by friends, Kate and I were invited to join Skipper Vince, mainsheet trimmer Brian, fore trimmers Shawn and Megn on bow. Kate and I managed the foredeck and kites as best we could, though we learned quick that 30-knot gusts will toss a J105 fast. We weren't at the sheets when we broached; we were just holding on.

Ten or more boats on the start line, and the 105s had first start. We popped the kite five minutes in and were hitting 13 knots in the gusts until we took a big one, broached, and couldn't get control before the kite ripped. Back under jib until we could raise the smaller spare.

The rain came and went as we rail-meated the boat and stayed as warm as we could. The skipper and crew had raced this course more times than I've raced any course, and knew the islands, straits, winds, and tides. We took the inside rounding Orcas and cut closer to San Juan as most of the pack took the long arm up near Canada.

As we cut close to the lighthouse off a private elk-hunting island, a rainbow came out and we could see Roche Harbor and the distant committee boat. Light on the wind, with the bigger boats easing past, we ambled over the line: 4th in our class, 12th overall. Not bad for a blown kite and two new crew.

We entered Roche Harbor and its overlooking towers, plugged in the heater, and found some warmth after a November race in the PNW.
