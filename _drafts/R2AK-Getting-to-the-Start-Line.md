The Race to Alaska is insane. Port Townsend to Ketchikan, seven hundred and fifty miles, no motor, most of it through Canada. We had heard about it for years, maybe as far back as 2012, and somewhere along the way it crossed over from "those people are insane" to "this is on the checklist." The race is its own story. This is about how an ordinary group of friends got to the start line, which is the part nobody tells you about, and the part that decides whether you go at all.

You don't take on something audacious by being brave about it. You take it on by climbing a ladder of smaller things, each one big enough to be real and small enough to survive, until the audacious thing is just the next rung.

For us the bottom of the ladder was Duck Dodge. Tuesday night on Lake Union, a two-drink minimum, everybody welcome, a chaos fest with a theme and a pile of stickers at the end and nobody keeping real score. Safety matters, the rest is a party, and crews are always looking for one more body. If you don't come in through a school or something formal, this is the door into sailing in Seattle. From there you climb. Friday beer-can races and the Ballard Cup out of Shilshole, then the slightly more serious Corinthian Yacht Club races on Wednesdays, then the longer weeknight races run by any number of clubs. You do your short stuff, then you do your long stuff, and the big races people actually train for come in June and July: the offshore series down in Oregon, Van Isle 360 around Vancouver Island, and Race to Alaska. One day the long stuff is 750 miles to Ketchikan.

We met the way you meet, on a boat at Duck Dodge, and the plan started over beers. Find the right boat, buy it or rent it, figure out the team. It came together as five: Kate and me, two friends from the racing scene, and Natalie, who we had raced with and who rounded the crew out the way we wanted it, balanced. Once the five of us were aligned we did the unglamorous things that make it real, a Venmo, money down, a signed entry.

We found the boat through Team Wild, also called Monkey Fist, who had built *Wildfire* at the Port Townsend Wooden Boat Festival, which is also where Race to Alaska finishes and hands out its awards. We wrote a contract with them for full-time use of *Wildfire* from May, the race in June, and all of July to get her back. It is the kind of document that feels awkward to write between friends and saves the friendship later. I raced with the Team Wild crew a few times in March and April to learn the boat, and then it was our team, and May was a wall of work.

*Wildfire* is a thirty-foot ultralight, which for this race is a sensible size, smaller than you would guess and a step up from a Santa Cruz 27. Balsa core, fiberglass, a lead keel, and not much else. Spartan. Living aboard is a lot like camping. We cooked almost everything on a Jetboil or a small stove, freeze-dried dinners, cheese and bars and candy for the watches. Kate and Natalie ran the provisioning.

Everyone took a piece of the boat. We hauled her out and surveyed her, and redid some of the rigging, which turned out to be half a project until it snapped on the way up, which is a story for another time. We still had to get her to the start, so we motored her north, and we ran a three-day shakedown toward Port Townsend to get the whole crew moving as one. We meant to go further and turned back early on the tide, which was funny, and also exactly the kind of thing you want to learn before the start and not after it.

That is the part I would recommend to anyone. Not the race, the getting ready. You learn more from coordinating people, managing the projects, and running small experiments over months than from almost anything else, and by the time you reach the start line the audacious thing has quietly turned ordinary. You have already done most of it. The race is another story.
