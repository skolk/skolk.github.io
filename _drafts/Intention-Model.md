My wife and I did a deconstruction of the values and efforts I've built over the years, to understand more about the model I didn't know I had for approaching the world. We were using the book [*Designing Your Life*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Designing+Your+Life+Burnett+Evans), but to be fair Kate is also a Designer at IDEO, and her internal touch pulled insights out of me that I definitely wouldn't have perceived if I had done this on my own. While it would be great to find your own Human-Centered Designer for your self-deconstruction, it might be easier to just start with a good friend, or an objective relation.

The book is looking for ways to prototype and amass experience that is tangible rather than normative or aspirational. As much as you say you want to write a novel or become an artist, what is it that you already do, and have committed to? In a subtle way it's suggesting that you do the things you do for a reason, and you can change that reason, but it's easier to flow down the stream and make subtle turns than to pick a new river.

We wrote out the projects that I loved working on, the ones I felt the most in flow in, the ones that brought me the most success, and the times I learned the most. We also explored which ones were the opposite: where I felt the most stress, the most downtrodden, the most trapped, and where I learned the least.

The point of the exercise wasn't to show what to focus on, but what I had done, and how it had turned out. It was a deconstruction of the past to better understand where the rocky road was and where the fast track is. We made hundreds of Post-it notes, and then began grouping them into categories that seemed similar.

The act of using sticky notes was important, because when we grouped them it wasn't always a match, or it brought up points about them that we might or might not stay with. If they had been a drawing there would have been erasing everywhere, and a hesitancy to play with the ideas.

Kate, my partner, is good at this. She does it for a living; I hadn't been subject to it before, and to be honest I don't think the book we were going through would have been 'enough' on its own to convey how difficult it can be to go through the synthesis process.

She came up with several models, many of which, once assembled, we agreed didn't resemble a reality we could agree on, so we scrapped them.
