A friend invited me on a 12-mile, 7,500-vertical-foot day, and I was more than intimidated. We talked it through, and I loaded the van to head out to the pass. Knowing I couldn't make the three-hour drive from the 5am ferry, I left the night before and stopped at a Safeway to stock up on the massive amount of sugar I'd need to compensate for my lack of training.

I had last toured in early April in the Alps, used to 3,000 to 5,000 feet of vert with 20 kilos on my back. Two months of sailing and bouldering since then, and my mountain legs had gone soft.

At 6:40am it took about five seconds for a friend to decide we should go 15 miles and 7,000 feet (good?), so we dashed down a muddy road with fallen trees, and hit at least one pool over eight inches deep. The truck steamed when I stopped. Couldn't bother with a potential cracked radiator when there was a mountain to climb.

We set out on the White Pine Trail, aiming for Mt. Chiwaukum. I realized very quickly that even my base hiking speed wasn't enough to hold conversation on the flats, and any vertical was going to hurt. We caught up at stream crossings and talked about finding mountain partners, sustainability, synthetic biology, and travel.

Five hours to get above treeline and reach the base bowl, snow and avalanche sprawl around us. The others were far ahead by then. I was cooked, maybe 4,500 feet of gain, and decided to enjoy the view.

Heard a slide while they were pushing higher. Saw two dots head back down. We regrouped, found a route down, sloshed the spring melt, crossed sketchy streams on skis, and made the seven miles back to the car. Wet shoes included.
