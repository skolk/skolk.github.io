We slipped out of Shilshole at three in the morning, the boat heavy with three weeks of food and a list of things I hoped we had fixed.

I had left my job that January, and an empty calendar does something to the imagination. Alaska stopped being a someday and turned into a this-year. The plan was not to leap at it. The plan was to take *Petrichor*, our Catalina 36, up the Inside Passage first, as a delivery. A delivery is meant to be the dull part, the repositioning you grind out so the real trip can begin somewhere better. This one ran the other way. The delivery was where the boat got sorted, the crew got built, and the route got learned. It was not practice for Alaska. It was most of Alaska.

The first step was smaller still. Earlier in the spring we ran the boat up to Princess Louisa Inlet, to the falls at the head of it, north past Pender Harbour. We went early in the season, which meant cold, and we told the two friends who came, both of whom had sailed with us in Mexico and farther, exactly that: it is going to be cold. We motored most of the way, tied up a night in False Creek under the towers by Granville, then pushed on through the dark in snow and a little rain and fog. It was beautiful up there. One other boat in the whole inlet. We came home, swapped crew, sailed the Gulf Islands and the San Juans, and came back with a list. *Petrichor* needed a new battery system. She needed insulation. She needed heat. A dozen small systems wanted sorting before she could go north for real.

So we did the work, all of it, in the same months we were getting a second boat ready for Race to Alaska. Two boats, two punch lists, a lot of flying around and managing the parts. None of it felt like getting ready. It felt like the trip had already started, in the boatyard, months before we cast off.

In early May we cast off for the long leg, three weeks to Ketchikan. I brought crew I trusted: David and Remi, who I had mostly skied with, but who turned out to be Sea Scouts, which is another way of saying reliable in a boat at three in the morning. Three of us stood watches, an hour on and two off, so there was always a rested body when you needed one.

Day one carried us all the way through the San Juans and across into Canada, forty or fifty miles, to Pender Island. Then a stop in Nanaimo to top up the groceries, and after it the long day: Nanaimo up through Campbell River and into Seymour Narrows, twenty hours of it, to a tight anchorage with a billygoat of an inlet off Johnstone Strait. Somewhere in there we fell in with Luna, Cannon, and Barb, who had taken second in Race to Alaska on an Olson 30 and now cruised a Catalina 36 like ours. They looked like Kate and me in twenty or thirty years, and I was fine with that.

We took a rest day in the Broughtons, off Blackfish, then crossed to Port Hardy and changed the crew over. Another sailor flew up from Seattle to take the next legs, and the two of us set off on a sixty or seventy mile day before we picked up Christina, who had been with us at Princess Louisa, in Bella Bella.

From there it was the inside of the inside. North through Klemtu, staying off the outer coast because we were motoring and the protected channels gave us something the open water would not. Foggy inlets, walls of cloud, a set of hot springs we climbed into off the boat. It was a different trip than the one you would have under sail in the sun, and I would not trade it. We worked up to Prince Rupert, swapping batteries the whole way and chasing an electrical fault I have since understood and would handle differently. Then the last of it, the crossing to Ketchikan.

That was the delivery, the small step that turned out to be most of the trip. What the boat and the crew did once we were actually up there in Alaska, that comes later.
