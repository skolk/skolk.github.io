Kate and I drove three hours from Nelson to Revelstoke on frost-glazed roads. Canadian roads require winter tires from October to May, so any slippage was on us. We had all-wheel drive and watched cars spinning out on ice where we were fine, but the sheen of guilt was there.

Kate's skis had been having trouble with warm snow sticking to the skins. We bought glop stopper and kept learning how cold snow, warm snow, and wax interact.

Revelstoke hadn't changed much since 2019. We met up with long-time friends and recounted the past few years: skiing, hunting, moving away from drinking culture in the mountains.

The way up through Rogers Pass was gentle until it wasn't. 1,000 feet over two miles, then 2,000 feet over one mile. Packs heavy with Christmas dinners, the going slow. On the way up we passed a cheerful trio of Norwegians who greeted us from their downhill run and promised to meet us at the hut.

When we arrived, the hut was empty. We had time to situate and claim bunks before other parties shuffled in as the light waned.

The Norwegians took a table with us and a group of Nelson and Revelstoke locals. We slowly warmed to each other and began sharing food, singing "Silent Night" in three languages, and talking about the snowpack and plans for the next few days.

We connected on ideas about experience being at your whim or you being at its, the grace of getting out there, and where our journeys had taken us. Games we used to obliquely gather closer together pulled out conversations about mistranslation and aliveness: what we were celebrating in each other in that warm cabin on a cold night with clear views of the stars.

I couldn't help a verse of "Here's a Health to the Company" coming out.

Until we meet again, new friends.
