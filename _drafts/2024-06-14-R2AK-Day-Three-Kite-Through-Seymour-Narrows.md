Our watch schedule put us on four hours of sailing, four hours of rest, and two hours floating between crew maintenance, sailing assistance, and potential rest if all duties were done.

I woke after a rough night of sailing until 0200 to find a wind shift and the crew ready to set the kite. We'd made a lot of progress and could make more. We had the jib up and spinnaker sheets still set from the day before. A quick line check and we were ready. It was blowing 15 knots and gusting higher. Going to be sporty.

As we raised the black all-purpose symmetrical spinnaker, I could see us closing in on a J24 that had been sailing all night, along with the Hobie 16 that had been keeping pace with all the monohulls. We'd been doing 6 to 7 knots under jib and slowly watched our speed rise into the 8-to-9-knot range, touching 10 to 11 when we caught a wave and surfed down.

Unfortunately for the boats that had made ground on us the previous night, Seymour Narrows is a hell of a tide gate. There was no fighting 7 knots of current, and it was dangerous to try.

We made 30 miles in 4 hours, dousing the kite at Campbell River to gybe around complicated outflows, then raising it again when the current was with us through Seymour. The R2AK media boat caught up as we entered the notorious passage.

Went below for a rest before what I knew would be a long pedal leg. But that's for tomorrow.
