Kate and I have sailed together for almost five years, about half of those on our own boat. Of all the stressful situations we encounter, the most stressful are in marinas around other people's boats.

This past week we had to take Petrichor off a shipping vessel, start her without much time to check the systems, and put her in a new marina with a new crew and, to our surprise, a new tie-up system.

After over 200 days on the water, we finally came to the conclusion that how we were taught to dock is unproductive.

The bow person steps off. If they go too far forward, the boat can't stop. If there's a wind off the dock and the pilot reverses, the boat gets pushed away. This happens too often. Ideally the bow and stern lines are held by different people who step off about two-thirds forward and stop the boat between them, coordinating to step off at the same time while watching the captain. It gets a little too complicated, and I think most of that bow/stern-lines-only approach comes from smaller motorboats, which can turn into the dock with only their bow. Less possible with a keel and fixed prop.

The solution: use a midship cleat for the first tie. Bring in the boat, then use the bow or stern (via hanging lines off the safety lines, what some call "elephant ears") to decide which to tie off next (usually stern). This way the pilot can reverse without fear of leaving the dock, the dock person can stop forward and aft motion, and you're less likely to drift away.

All this seems obvious in retrospect. Moving away from lessons you thought you knew is hard. Just because someone gave you matching orders doesn't mean they work for your boat.

In similar fashion, Kate and I practiced man-overboard drills yesterday after the docking experiments, and found a few different approaches to a figure-eight versus a quick stop on our boat, which has different windage than the J24 we learned on.

Happy experimenting. Figure out your systems. Looking forward to a year of learning up here in the Salish Sea.
