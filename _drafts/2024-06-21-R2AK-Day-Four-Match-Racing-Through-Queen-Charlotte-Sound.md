"We're match racing Pickle Train and Rock the Boat!" That was the first thing I heard waking up. We had the number 1 up and full main. Team Rock the Boat was right off our port, no more than 100 feet away. All three boats tacking back and forth in the narrows, miles from the opening into the Broughtons.

The air was light and we were all scratching for wind. Pickle Train had jokingly called themselves a Santa Cruz 27 Max; they'd replaced almost all the major components, keel, rudders, mast, and reinforced the deck with Kevlar. They could point higher than us and Rock the Boat and had better sails than either of us. Our Olson 30, on the other hand, could bash in rough seas and fly downwind with the symmetrical spinnaker.

After a few glorious hours, we came out of the narrows and into Queen Charlotte Sound. The whispers of Pacific wind turned into a full conversation as we played with a mild swell on the nose, pointing for Cape Caution. Gaining cell signal between Alert Bay, Telegraph Cove, and Port Hardy, we checked in on the rest of what we'd soon be calling the "kids' table" and saw they were taking wider angles. We passed our companion boats with a more direct route.

The sun grew lower as we neared Cape Caution. Weather showed lighter conditions on the inside and breezy on the outside. We'd seen a catamaran ahead take the outside route, and the previous year's winning team had told us to "always find wind," so we leaned further out.

We heard Pickle Train on VHF communicating with a tug about their intentions to pass. We'd take the outside route via Fitz Hugh Channel. That was the last we heard of them for the next few hours as the sun set.
