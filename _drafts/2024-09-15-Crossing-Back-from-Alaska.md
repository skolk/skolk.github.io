We just crossed the border heading south, raised and lowered the Q flag, and found a slip. That's the last Alaska we'll see this summer.

Alaska has been on the list ever since we decided to move to the Pacific Northwest three years ago, both R2AK and the wilds beyond. The race was a grand adventure of fellow travelers on the same path, all helping each other against the long struggle, with "race" as a time-bounding condition rather than the point. The fjord explorations, cove-jumping, and town stops after the race were less bounded and led in different directions. We found fishermen, hikers, sailors, bakers, and brewers, all living their lives in the north, more connected to the land and sea than even what we find in Seattle. Salmon runs come up in casual conversation, and the ever-present XtraTuf brown boots are the everyday wear in both big and small towns, the rain and mud inescapable when you live in a rainforest.

As usual, the slower we go and the further we tread off the path, the more curious the creatures and people we find. Beyond the cruise ships lie the slow sailors catching coho and pinks to share around a campfire, or an invitation to hike up a rocky mound in bear-loved country to share a view of rolling fog as humpbacks feed around your anchored boats in small coves.

It's not done, but it is over. Kate and I have seen what it means to take a summer with 4 am sunrises and 11 pm sunsets on the water with nothing to do but what's in front of you.

We don't wonder if we'll be up here again. We do wonder how we'll return, what waters we'll step into next time, and how our feet will be different then. Already we know more about the fish runs and the glacial melting. But what comes next, whom will we bring, and how will we bring them?
