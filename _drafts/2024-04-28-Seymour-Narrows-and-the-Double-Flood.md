When you learn to rest, you see how much further you can go. The suffering becomes bearable because it's temporary, and the mission gains momentum in each step.

We woke at 0200 and regretted sleeping before the sun rose. We'd covered a good distance on day one into the Gulf Islands and had gained some confidence in the motor's new cruising speed, picking up half a knot after a tune and hull clean. But out in the Strait of Georgia at 0300, bashing in waves with mixed moonlight and a bow watch going full time, my hopes were dropping for making our planned gate at Seymour Narrows. "One of the vilest stretches of water in the world," as Captain George Vancouver described it. The narrows can run up to 15 knots of current and claimed 120 vessels and 114 lives before Ripple Rock was demolished in 1958 at roughly $30M Canadian in today's dollars.

Here we were, headwind, back-current, dark skies, slim prospects of making the gate, and the real possibility of a 3 am alarm the next day for another attempt. I went below as the sun came up, normally the most optimistic time of day. My crewmate was smiling, simply satisfied to be on the water. My expectations had been arrogant, and I'd let them steal the joy of being present.

When I woke, the current was with us and we'd started to gain speed. And yet the current was always against you in the strait, something about the double flood: one current helping, another running hard against you. Remy had been here before and suggested we watch the water surface and passing boats. Sure enough, we kept our speed, edging in and out of the back-flow, staying in the favorable band.

It isn't over until it is, and Seymour had more to say before we were through.
