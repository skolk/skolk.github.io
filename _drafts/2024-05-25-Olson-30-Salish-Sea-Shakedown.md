Last week Kate and I took the Olson 30 out into the Salish Sea without a gas motor, using the pedal drives we'd modified to mount on the transom. We sailed out of the slip with some help and ran downwind in relative ease the 30-plus miles to Langley. It was our double-hander test in prep for a race the following week. We swapped sail, reefed the main, and learned to balance the ultralight 30-footer. We had a good 15 knots at our back more or less the whole time, and as far as sailing goes, we took the day in strides.

After an evening with friends and family, we set off the next morning under pedal power, upwind from Langley Marina, testing our maneuverability in 5 knots of wind, and were soon sailing upwind in the breeze. What we hoped would be a 15-knot day turned into a 20-knot constant headwind, with gusts in the 25s and occasional 30s.

We put in our third reef and reduced our headsail to the smallest we had (our storm jib was at the loft for repairs), and worked our way around the inlets and ferry routes. We tried several methods of holding power off: slacking the main or jib in different combinations to reduce heel while keeping one of us on the high side to keep the boat as level as we could. It wasn't until we figured out feathering (a term we learned later) that our little piece of chaos turned into a manageable passage home. We'd point as high as the tensioned foresail would allow in a gust, let off ever so slightly, less than 3 degrees, then head up again, never letting the sails haul us off course and always ready to push higher into a lift.

This wasn't close to relaxing, but it became our routine over the 9 hours it took to cross the 4-to-6-foot waves on the bow, with no more than 5 minutes below deck to boil water for a meal or pee in a jar (lest we fall overboard going off the rail).

It started as a Type 1 activity, progressed into Type 3, then leveled into Type 2 once we put the effort into riding it out.

May you face the challenges before you.
