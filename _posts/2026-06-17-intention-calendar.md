Every solar quarter (solstice and equinox) I take a day to reflect over the past quarter and plan for the next one, and in general the upcoming months. I use a combination of methods I've derived from [Zig Ziglar](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ae-VJ_lauCw), [SMART goals](https://www.projectsmart.co.uk/smart-goals.php), [stretch goals](https://hbr.org/2017/01/the-stretch-goal-paradox), and [brainstorming](https://www.ideou.com/pages/brainstorming). I then post my Intention Calendar online and share it with my friends, letting some know about plans I have and inviting them.

## How I came to think about goal setting

The first time I realized I could plot a course for life was 2007. I was working in a used camping gear shop just off Georgia Tech's campus in Atlanta, where I had opened an Outside Magazine and was reading a story on 10 people who were "living their dream life." I don't remember what most people were doing, but the one that caught me was the story of a director of the IRC (the International Rescue Committee) who spent his time flying all over the world from earthquakes to floods to civil wars, helping people get where they needed to go, and the supplies they needed to keep going.

I looked up what it took to get to his position, and remember three qualifications: must be fluent in French (and English), drive a stick shift, and have lived in a third world country for at least 5 years. I signed up for a French class, asked my roommate to teach me how to drive her stick shift, and applied to a student group that had work opportunities in Nairobi.

One year later, in a cafe in Nairobi: confused, and frustrated. I had worked with local business, learned from entrepreneurs and students in slums how they navigated life, fought policies with the group I was working with, and started a micro revolt by forming an ad hoc union with my fellow interns (14 of us from 9 countries). It was hectic, chaotic, and wonderful. But my internship was almost up, and I didn't know where to go next, or how to get there. Then a friend dropped a book in my metaphorical lap about working less and getting more (the four hour work week), which sounded like a cheap trick, but the core of the book was helpful in itself.

One of the models in the book was derived from a self help speaker known as Zig Ziglar: Be, Do, Have. Define what you want to be, what you want to do, what you'd like to have. Then work backwards as to how to get it. Zig's model stressed accountability, deadlines, and a systematic process (once you create a system that lets you succeed even when you fail, you've already won). It was novel for me, and oddly simple.

What do you want to have been, have done, and had by… the end? Start working backwards, list as many things as you want. Some are giant (a house! An island! To be a Mom!) others are simple: I want to travel to Paris.

Here I was in Nairobi, 21, with 2000 USD cash in my pocket, a credit card, a backpack, and the realization that I had seen more of Kenya via local buses and ex-pat parties than I had seen of my own state, playing it safe staying on a university campus. The short result is that I went overland via public transportation and hitch hiking from Nairobi to Cape Town, hiking, swimming, sailing, climbing. The long lasting effect is that I started to systematize my plans. The end of your life is too far off, but planning for it gives you understanding of your heading and the steps you should take to begin getting there: it's not helpful to have 500 items on a list. It is helpful to have 3. So I started charting. I made a list of what I want in life, what I want in 10 years, then in 5 years, then in 1 year, then broke up 1 year into 4 quarters.

Sometimes the year break down is big enough that I need quarterly goals (saving to buy a car or go on a trip), sometimes they're seasonally related (go skiing, or see the wildflower bloom) and are items on a calendar. The result is that I end up with a year out (at minimum) calendar of planning. EVERY quarter I focus on the Equinox and Solstice to redo it, but that's up to you. Once that calendar is made (and again, they change every quarter, your course is never set, but it's necessary to plan that far out to start moving in that direction) I work it.

## A note on brainstorming

Brainstorming isn't actually a great way to make a decision, but it is a great way to get creative about an area to see options that you didn't realize were there. I've learned through working with designers that brainstorming requires mutability. You can't work with ideas etched in stone. Sticky notes solve this problem by allowing you to take an idea and move it around, group it with others, and easily throw it away without destroying your entire work.

## What to use this on

I use this calendar primarily for athletic pursuits and travel. I've had friends use it for professional reasons, and in general I find it creates continuity in my head to see a year in a circle.

## Start with the end in mind

What's your end game? I'll give you a constraint: one day you will die, and you'll probably have a few regrets. What don't you want to regret? What are the huge, wild, insane items that you want to see in your limited time here? Summit Everest? Sail the world? Free climb El Cap? Ski the Alps? Write those down on sticky notes.

I enjoy the framework: what do you want to have been? What do you want to have learnt? What do you want to have had?

I've seen this executed differently by everyone I've encountered, so I won't give too much guiding, but they have to be (to my engineering mind) measurable. "At peace" is hard. "Kind" is hard. But "having done X" or "started Y" is tangible.

## Break it down

Now that you have your life list, break it down into the next 10 years, 5 years, and 1 year, and how some amount of effort you can put into each of these life items.

A useful model for actions is the pyramid: 10 A items build up to 6 B items built up to 3 C items allow for 1 D item to happen. In running this would be a large amount of 5k's give way to half marathons and then marathons and then ultras.

Alternatively the exponential saving model for, say, "I want to buy a house" is a breakdown of how much money you need in X time, and how much per year, month, week, and day you have to contribute. The "what does this cost" of time is also a factor in this way: the 10,000 hour rule.

Once you have your moonshot life goals and your ramp ups over 5 and 10 years (or 25, up to you), approach this year.

## Why do this often

Like I said earlier, I do this every quarter, and plan about a year in advance. For many of the trips and adventures my partner and I plan, this is necessary to reserve a sailboat or a cabin, and to get people to commit for large trips and expeditions takes a lot of effort.

I believe I saw the circle calendar in elementary school at some point and it just stuck in my head; but I cannot attribute it to any individual. A friend of mine told me he thought of years as a helix where you're seeing the year ascend into the next layer.

Either way it helps me see continuity that the boxed month/week calendar doesn't create. I also enjoy the circle because it shows transitions and continuity between seasons.

## Place Be, Do, Have at the center

Now that you have your Be, Do, Have for the year, place them in the center. I then break them down even further and place a Be, Do, Have in each quarter, which sometimes gets complicated because they don't always transition well.

Once you have it down to a quarter, you work out:

* what week or day you set for the goal to be accomplished.
* I then divide my weeks into the build up for gradual goals: say "run 10 miles" when the goal is a marathon.
* If the item isn't actionable, you're not going to do it.

## Be public

Creating accountability for yourself is by far the hardest part of this exercise. It's easy to put ideas on a sheet of paper, but they aren't given life until there's a date they need to be finished, and a person or group that is holding you to that idea.

The most difficult part of this system is accountability. It's actually not hard to say "I'm going to sail around the world" then not do it. It's much harder to commit to that, and give a friend $5000 and say "If I don't have a boat by next year, keep this. If I do, then give it back." (There are a few escrow systems like this, and you can make a different one, but you get the point.) Others have used accountability groups, or running clubs (systems, not goals).

I will confess I've made HUGE goals which didn't have enough accountability and therefore they flopped. I know when I plan a trip (I love planning sailing and climbing trips) that when I set a date and pay someone, I'm gonna do it. I believe that's why marathons with entry fees and a date are so popular compared to "well I'm gonna just go run my own marathon."

If this doesn't hit enough at the core, it could also help to go back to your values: I am helpful, therefore I want to help X number of people, or this community. I want to see more trees in the world so I'm going to join this club and plant these trees.

## Commit

So how do we achieve both the big goal and the little steps? We prioritize. I actually find most times that I put too much on the wheel, and have to narrow it down to no more than 3 big goals. Then I show up.

## The circle concept

I've always thought of the calendar as a circle. Maybe it was a calendar I saw in elementary school, I'm not sure, but it's how I've thought of it. With the New Year at the bottom, and Spring and Summer at the top, like a clock. So when I plan my years it looks like that. A friend told me he extrapolated it into a helix seen from two dimensions with the years flowing into each other. A calendar helps you see how much time any one item takes. I work in weeks and weekends: where if the goal I have can't be worked on once a week or weekend (lots of my goals are trips) then it's not approachable.

## One comment on systems

Quantity over quality leads to quality (draw 20 pictures to get to your 3 masterpieces).

Also Have: often I put Have "less" and list a few items I want to let go of.

## Plan like you need it

* Make a lot of plans
* Accept that you can only do one thing at a time
* Commit to a few of them
* Turn it into a system
* Set a due date
* Create accountability
* Get excited about it
* Put it on paper
* Tell those who matter
* One step a day

Let's have at it. Start at your own resolution: if planning the next 3 months is what helps you, great. If it's the next decade, well, ok.
