We often take friends out sailing for the first time, and I wanted to set some expectations about what a quick day sail will look like.

First off, our boat - SV Petrichor - is a Catalina 36. She has about three feet of freeboard, meaning the deck sits that far above the water, and she's diesel-powered. The day usually goes like this: meet at the boat at the agreed time, step on, get her ready, do a quick safety brief and check in to find out how everyone feels about being on the water, and then head out under power. With a diesel engine you exit the dock, motor out of the breakwater, and into Puget Sound, the waters around Seattle. We'll go out anywhere from two hours to multiple days, depending on what we've talked about, using both the sails and the motor depending on conditions, and using all the facilities on the boat - the galley, head, helm, and berths (kitchen, bathroom, steering wheel, and beds).

## Before you arrive

I'll send out a dock time and create a WhatsApp group so everyone can communicate. If we're at Shilshole Marina in Ballard, Seattle, there's a parking lot in the back with visitor parking indicated. We're near building M5, so park in that area and then come up to the gate we've indicated. Most gates have key fob systems, so either someone has to let you in or we'll come up with the key. Use the bathroom before you get to the boat if you can. There is a head on board, but it's easier to go on shore.

## What to bring

Depending on the time of year, dress for warmth. Bring closed-toed shoes, sunglasses, sunscreen, a rain or wind jacket, a sweater, and probably pants - it gets windy and cold even in the summer.

If you have a life jacket you like wearing, bring it, though we have them on board unless we've said otherwise.

Always bring food and snacks to share with the crew, even just for a single day. For multiple days, bring more. Chips, salsa, beer, non-alcoholic drinks, sandwiches - all appreciated. More complicated meals are possible, but things that don't require utensils are easiest. If in doubt, bring more and leave some behind.

## Once you're aboard

Ask permission to come aboard before stepping on. Put your things down in the cabin out of the way and store food that needs to be kept cold.

It's always appreciated to help get the boat ready: take off the window covers and the sail cover, get lines ready for raising sails, unplug the shore power, and disconnect the water hoses if they aren't filling anything up. These are just common practices.

Dock lines stay tied until we're almost ready to cast off, which doesn't happen until we've had a quick safety talk - life jackets on, knowing where the fire extinguishers, the radio, and the throwables are, and running through person-overboard drills. Then we go over our float plan: how we're going out today, what the conditions are, what to expect.

We start the motor, agree on who will help take the boat off the dock, and step on at the right moment. Lines come off and we head out.

Generally we don't take the fenders (the bouncy things on the side of the boat) off until we're all the way out into the strait, and we start putting dock lines away almost as soon as we're off the dock. We wait until it's clear we won't have to come back to the dock for any reason - that does happen.

On a moving boat you always want three points of contact. Two hands for the boat and one foot, or two feet and one hand. And in general, we wear life jackets unless you're sleeping down below.

Happy to go over other parts of operating the boat, but this is a good first introduction. More to come on raising sails, nouns and verbs on a boat, reading the weather and the wind, boat maintenance, trip planning, and what gear lives on board.
