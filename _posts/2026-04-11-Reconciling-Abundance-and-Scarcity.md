How do you balance acceptance and agency? One way is to be radically present - finding pain, joy, elation, or serenity in the storm, the turbulence, the calm. The other takes that situation, inserts desire, and acts to change it. One generates situations where you create rather than receive. The other rejects victimhood. In the deepest version of acceptance there is no drama; only what is.

I don't know if this is the core tension between techno-utopians, tool-crafters, creators, and agents of change, on one side, and local movements that want to work within firm limits and boundaries, on the other. Maybe there's a thread between the two, but figuring it out has been a challenge between urban productionists and rural land workers for millennia.

In my experience with thought workers - abstractionists, the people who take ideas and find where they apply in the world, inductive or deductive, looking for systems, frameworks, patterns, patterns of meaning - so much might exist that you find, or that you impose.

Contrast that with native groups I've talked with who've been of the land for time immemorial, watching what over-extraction causes and learning the boundaries it leads to. Understanding food systems, energy extraction, harvesting, wood and materials for shelter. Not viewing objects as resources, but working with verbs and language to be with rather than take from. And yet challenging some of that acceptance, seeing the limits of those lifestyles and how they end up with their own feudalistic tendencies and constraining identities. It is not a utopia, or if it is, it is one that is still exploring itself.

The advent of fire shifted humanity from other species, granting more movement and latitude, teaching us to sew fibers, introducing people to northern climates, and reducing the incidence of cold feet. Should I accept cold feet? Should I accept being cold and hungry? What is the cost of progress, and what is the reconciliation between the two different groups?

The one-to-many versus the many-to-many choices come up in writers like Elinor Ostrom - the idea of the commons, collaboration, what is internal and what is external, and how we frame those concepts so that we can work with and around them.
