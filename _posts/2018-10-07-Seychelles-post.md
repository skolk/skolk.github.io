Location: The Seychelles: Mahe, Praslin, Curious, La Digue, Silhouette.

Time: Sep-Oct 2018

Boat: SunSail 444

Skipper: Sean K.

Crew: Kate S. , Kate B. , Emily W. Dave C. , Gerald S. , Joni N. , Cami Z. , Sarah G. , Jane S.

2018.9.27: Fly to Mahe, The Boathouse and beach:

We flew from Nairobi to Mahe Victoria, less than a 3 hour flight; Transport is expensive in the Seychelles, so we rented a car to drive to our airbnb. oh, you MUST bring your vaccination card (or… have a picture of it?) if you’ve been living in a non western country for more than a week. Mahe is the largest island, the Capital; Victoria, is the smallest Capital in the world. The roads are well paved, and the people car aware. We were 10, and couldn’t find any accommodation for that many so 1/2 were in an air bnb up in the hills the other in a guest house closer to the beach, a five four split, our tenth to arrive later After arriving at our airbnb, we drove down to the beach (no one seemed to care where we parked) and found festival setting up and cocktails to be consumed. The Sun set behind Silhouette island.

2018.9.28: Go on a hike; find supplies.

The Next morning we split up, some going on a hike, the other groups found art galleries and searched for markets and supplies. On the Hike we saw Golden Orb spiders, Seychelles Flying Foxes, Tropic Birds, Bulbul’s, and Common Myna’s. The Hills of the Seychelles are lush with hard woods and tropics trees (Banana and ferns) interspersed with large Granite boulders. Despite a possible conclusions the Seychelles are not volcanic, but a rogue plate which drifted away from the African Continent 65 Million years ago; which also lacked Humans until the Portuguese found them while off course.

That night we had our first crew meeting at the air bnb on the Hill. We discusses organizing food, who would be helping with what, and what a possible sail plan might be.

2018.9.29: Board Boat; sleep in marina.

In the morning we drove to a dive shop and took the morning to dive off the NW coast of Mahe, we saw jellyfish, Reef sharks, Octopus, scorpion fish, and learned more about where to dive in Praslin. We then packed up our airbnb and drove to Eden island, found where to park, and began assembling food, checking in the 44 foot catamaran, and reviewing all the technical aspects. Eden island is an odd mixture of Mall and private resort. The mall attached the marina, and that's where we staged our gear while checking out the boat, drinking coffee, and returning rental cars.

By 5pm we realized we weren't going to make it out that night to the closest anchorage, and settled in for the night at the marina. We planned to have our first crew meeting, but had communication issues, between phones, radios, and a meet up point, and got drunk instead.

2018.9.30: Sail to Praslin.

We woke with the sun, Motored out of the marina, and to Ile Moyenne, a nearby marine park and snorkeling spot, we dropped anchor, and took the dingy out, but the water was choppy and the majority of the crew wanted to move on, some getting sea sick. We then set course for Praslin Island. Some +25Nm NE. The winds in the Seychelles are a consistent SE in October and we were on a broad or beam reach for over 4 hours.

The setup of the SunSail 444 was such that is could be more or less single handed with possible exception of raising the main, and even the there was a powered winch. The foot of the jib is angled so that it doesn't interfere with any of the crew on the trampoline, and the furler on the starboard next to the cockpit. There are two main sheets which run on top of the ridged sun canopy above the stern galley, one acts as a vang while on luff, and works quite well as a preventer while jibing (which we never did). In fact we failed to tack or jib the entire trip due to favorable winds for our direction of travel). The auto pilot was dialed in so that over correction of erratic turns we not a problem, and I was never in fear of leaving the helm for a second to grab a radio. My only issues with the rigging was the lazy jacks consistently hindered rising the main, catching on the battens. We sailed with one reef in at all times, the winds almost never less than 10 kts and sometimes peaking around 18kts.

Kate and I watched the the Noddy’s and the Frigate birds dance through the water occasionally seeing a feeding happen from above. The rare flying fish breaching. We sailed around the NW tip of Praslin before we were in the wind shadow, and dropped sails to motor into a NW facing cove with several other cats. Some of the crew went to shore to walk the beach, others prepared dinner.

We had been warned to always lock up our dingy, and take our toys on board, there had been reports of theft, we did this every night but never encountered an issue.

The majority of the anchorages are aggressively shallow, going from +30m to 5m in quick manner. The sandy bottoms were never an issue, but it did mean many boats were crowded along the shore, and there was never a lack of a cat next to us at any anchor.

2018.10.1: Praslin -Curious - Praslin: the bay (met up with dave)

We sailed for Curious, an old Leper colony off the NW shore of Praslin, where we encountered our first mooring, whom we had an entertaining half hour trying to loup in choppy waters. Once we double looped it (there wasn’t any top attachment point) half the crew went ashore, and half settled down to reading. On Shore is a doctor's museum, a short hike, and a large colony of giant tortoise. We also found mudskippers, ghost crabs, sun birds, and orb spiders.

From Curious we intended to head towards Bay St. Anne, but then decided to redirect to a spot closer to the dive shops. We anchored in a marine park behind a island restaurant, with some of the crew heading to shore to find drinks and pick up our last crew member (Dave!), who had had his spear gun confiscated at customs, and had flown a island jumper to Praslin from Mahe. Sean and Kate also found a dive shop to coordinate for diving the next day.

Around this time we also decided that the ship water wasn’t worth drinking, and that we we were using too much water washing dishes and washing hands in the bathroom. We agreed to stop bathroom hand washing (we had sanitizer and soap). Bring a bucket of salt water to wash dishes, then rinse in fresh water after, and cese any shower taking.

2018.10.2: Praslin: Sean, Kate, and Sarah dive, we take boat to marine park.

In the morning, Sean, Kate, and Sarah were dropped off by Joni on the beach and went to dive with Octopus Divers, we saw several reef sharks, stingrays, and Eagle Rays. The rest of the crew went to shore to hike on Paraslin, saw waterfalls, the world's largest coconut, and hiked the main port.

In the evening we motored over to a marine park and saw squid! Then anchored back in the same spot.

2018.10.3: Praslin to La Digue (storm)

We had a late start to the day (so… 7am!) , but motored out into the wind towards La Dige. One the way a storm rolled in. Moderate rain, and 20 kt winds. We debated turning into a local port, but it was West facing and I thought it would just put us in greater proximity to risk. We did have one forward hatch turn out to not me fully shut, and one of the cabins had about 20L of water dumped on the bed. we motored through the storm, which settled as we neared La Digue. We dropped anchor with all the other cats avoiding waves. And went ashore.

Some of the crew took the kayaks and paddle boards to a nearby beach and some of us went to town. La Digue has even less cars than Mahe, and most of the population seems to ride bikes. We (Sean, Kate, Sarah, and Jane). we walked through the town, and to a Vanilla plantation which also host one of the world's most beautiful beaches, where there were at least three weddings. On the way back we stopped in a bar, and discusses colonialism in Kenya with Jane (who’s been in Kenya for 70 Years) over Pina Coladas. There was much rejoicing.

The ship is equipped with an anchor drag alarm, which is useful, but difficult to judge both where you truly dropped the anchor, what your swing radius is, and in our case how much chain you let out (there were no markings, we should have marked the chain). Thus on this particular night the anchor alarm went off, Gerald, Emily, and Dave woke to make a decision, decided to wake me. I looked around saw that we were neither close to other boats, or the shore, and looking at our GPS track didn’t show signs of dragging the anchor (meaning we had set the anchor radius either too small or in the wrong place. This happened… one more time the same night with the same conclusion, but since this is a major concern, it's better to have happen than not. After the 2nd alarm went off I decided to sleep on deck. The last alarm to go off was a voltage meter (the boat was set up to run a 12v freezer and refrigerator), which is… too many alarms that aren't important enough, so we turned the icy things off. That was 5am (the 4th), it was going to be a long next day.

2018.10.4: La Digue to Silhouette.

Silhouette is a private island owned by the Hilton, named after the French finance minister of the 1800 somethings. The outline portraits are named after him, as is the term now, because they portraits were a cheap version and he was blamed for people being poor, much like Hoovervilles in the US.

We sailed from La Digue to Silhouette, we had a call ahead to reserve our spot, and sailed in high winds (+18 kts) on a broad reach, dropped anchor close to shore on a semi-protected cove, realized waves were braking too close, so motored away, and found our night stay. The cove was close to another cat, so we dropped a 2nd anchor to prevent swinging into them, when another boat showed up, who it turned out were friends of the 1st cat. The Three of us snugged in, and our crew relaxed into the water, beach, hike.

2018.10.5: Silhouette to Marine park by Mahe.

Kate and I awoke at 6am to hike the 3 miles to the hotel in the morning, looking at a variety a birds on the way. The Hilton was very confused by us, since almost no on is on the island but guests who’ve been flown there, and they didn’t know how to accept our money, so we had free coffee. We hiked back to breakfast on the boat, not before being thrown off our kayaks and losing some items. We lazily left our cove to sail back to Mahe, avoiding a Military boat and learning about GPS/Radio signaling (go away). To drop anchor a mear 5nm from Eden island. Watching the container ships bob about.

I think some of the crew went to shore, who’s to say.

2018.10.6: Boat is back in Eden Island

In the morning we anchored up, and motored into Eden island. The Marina wasn’t responding , so we took an easy slip and began unloading the boat. We couldn’t check in until 3pm, so we rented another two cars to store items in (and later explore), while disembarking. We checked in a little early, everyone had showers, got used to the air conditioning, and basically passed out.

2018.10.7:Fly out at 4pm

We split into two groups, on going to church, the other exploring spice museums. We saw over 100 spice plants. The islands was a French attempt to shortcut the Dutch Spice, it was mostly successful at growing, but never made it to scale (not sure why). There was cinnamon, nutmeg, vanilla, allspice, pepper, and many more.

We made our way to the airport around noon to have coffee, return cars, and realize on crew member (Cami!) missed her flight by 24 hours. While dealing with that, Dave got his spear gun back, the crew said our goodbyes, and flew two Three different continents.

Logistics notes:

- Taxis are expensive, we rented a car for 50 Euro a day, a 30 min taxi cost more than that.
- Restaurants are also somewhat expensive, but groceries are not, there’s also very good street food.
- Produce is somewhat lacking in general on the island.
- The trip cost somewhere around $1400+ per person without flights for 10 days total.
