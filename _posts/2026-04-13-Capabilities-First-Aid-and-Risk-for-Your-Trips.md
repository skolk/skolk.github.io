Salt was running a first aid class with several new sailors, many of whom were thinking about what it means to be out in deeper waters, further from healthcare and help. What started as a capabilities conversation - what can I do, what tools do I need - became a risk management conversation. What does it mean to take someone out who has a condition like epilepsy, or who is in constant need of medical care? What tools do you need versus how much risk are you trying to undertake? If somebody has a twisted ankle or is in a constant state of dehydration, that's a real burden. The difference between "can you take care of yourself" and "can you take care of someone else" and "can you manage the boat" is always something you're juggling.

A single-hander once told me that the difference between single-handing and double-handing is that you can sleep while somebody else steers the boat. That's infinitely more help. But at the same time, double-handing really just means switching off between two people doing single-handing - one person down, one person up, one person for the boat, one person for everyone else.

What I'm really proposing is that after you take a first aid class, and before you go on a big trip, you should take your what-if conversations extremely seriously. Today I watched several people work through theoretical situations. Some were probably more hyperbolic than others. Some were very real - like, you trip and fall on the bow. How do you get back to the cockpit, and what do you do if it's the bigger of you who needs to do that?

I see a lot of couples, and a lot of sailors in general, who want to de-risk everything but then don't look at the most common things that happen. The common things are mostly personal care: dehydration, arguments, sunburns. And then there are very serious situations - somebody falls into the water and it isn't clear how, or who, or what could happen next. If it's the larger person of a couple in the water and the smaller one on deck, walk through that scenario.

A lot of the point of the first aid class is to de-risk by practice rather than theory. The what-if-isms and the armchair-isms are dangerous in their own way because they let you think you have control over a situation, instead of just going out and experimenting. Jump in the water. Try your man overboard. Learn about your hoisting and getting people in and out. Put on your wetsuit. Test it, get out there, try things. Learn by doing.
