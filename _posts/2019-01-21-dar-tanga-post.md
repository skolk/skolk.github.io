Intro:

It had been on the long list to join a coastal race, which I imagine exists in San Francisco, but I hadn’t managed to participate in over my past 5 years of sailing. Coastal races are generally described as less than 50 nautical miles from the shore, where the distinction lies in weather or not you can receive assistance in a reasonable amount of time; blue water races or crossings would be ‘offshore’ races.

I was introduced to Brenden though his step mother whom I met on a farm in Naivasha Kenya, She told me he was looking for sailors to join him in a race around Zanzibar to the town of Tanga, about a 250 mile race that has been going over 50 years.

The Dar es Salaam Yacht club is over a 100 years old, they have lasers and Hobi cats on shore and all the keel boats are moored out around the cove. There were maybe 100 boats. The club itself has two restaurants, storage and a sizable beach. Three of us came by the airport through downtown Dar by taxi to the short.

Dec 5th:

We made our way to the ramp where a Dingy picked us up and we headed to the ship. With 11 inches of extra free board, it’s one of the more difficult boats I’ve ever climbed up on, using a fender for a step up, we made out way onto the yellow and blue hulled sloop. Brenden had acquired Vaka Lele a few months prior from a German expat who had sailed the globe twice with her after building the boat himself in New Zealand. At 35' feet, the Morgan based plans came with two berths, a hallway kitchen, galley, and sizable head.

After leaving our belongings in the hull, we returned to land to search for provisions. I enjoy cooking on ships, and made it a point to get enough veggies to make soups, and have raw slices for lunch. Brenden had premade curries and stews; we had both a cold box and a freezer hooked up to the solar panels in the never ending effort to find of the blanket of humidity that only let up under the sea breeze.

<img src="/images/blog_posts/image10.jpg" width="100%">

oh one more thing, that Brenden forgot to mention as were prepared; the engine isn't working and Brendan's boat boy (who’s name I never got, they only spoke in swahili to each other) was working on the starter. We joked that it was a sailing race, not a cruising race (a concept which implications hadn’t quite dawned on me.)

In the Race there are two classes, Cruiser (use your motor) and Open (no motor, start to finish). You can be bumped from open to cruiser class if you turn your motor on, even in an emergency. This means no matter how light the wind, or heavy the current, unless we were equipped to paddle; we would be undersail.

Not being fully able to utilize your toolkit of options is a great experience in detracting. I once drove a car for 6 months without reverse (the transmission had more or less exploded) I had to put it in neutral, get out, and push. Sailing without a motor isn’t that bad, but it does change the level of awareness required to make decisions in light wind, around other boats, and lessen the margin for error a captain can accept. We’d learn more about that later on in the trip.

We drove around Dar looking for a variety of veggies, meats, and cheeses. No one place seemed to satisfy all of our cravings, with one market having an abundance of veggies and no fruit, and the other just the opposite. We were planning for six people for 4 days on the boat, one on shore resupply. Two of the dinners were ‘maybe’ we were glad we packed them later.

Around four in the afternoon we were introduced to the rest of the race participants; there were 23 ships competing, some less than 25 feet, others above 40. All in all that's about 100 people. The rules of the race were reviewed, and tropical shirts with logos handed out. The Yacht club atmosphere of salty sailors and preppy white pants is always a strange mix, and makes for conversation grounded in

Dec 6th :

The next morning, Brendan and I had slept on Vukulele, Brendan climbed the mast and we threaded wires for the masthead light, while I explored the ships electrical puzzle. The motor still wasn’t working, and we grew closer and closer to 11.

The whole crew, Mandy and Manual joining us later in the morning, was settling in, wires were being arranged, the motor finally given up around 10:30, and ship were heading out towards the start.

The first leg of the race, which in theory was from Dar to Tanga anyway you could be about it; was to head west around the south tip of Zanzibar, head up the east coast, avoid fishing nets, and make our way between Zanzibar and Pemba on a north west corse for Zanzibar.

<img src="/images/blog_posts/image15.jpg" width="100%">

After the horn when off at 11am, 23 boats spilled off the line. Several making better way than us, and others, the cats, taking the long tack off. We began experimenting with our inner stay to make a staysail, and having a go at clipper rigging our ship. The next 6 hours were a battle against the wind and waves making up, and falling behind, with the sun setting at our backs.

I began cutting onions at 6 and sautéing with butter for an hour, while getting the curry going. We were trying to keep most items in one pot, but with rice it was nearly impossible, cooking at 20-30˚ is no fun task, and the kitchen was functionally in a hallway. We ate well past sunset while on a beam reach, and fell asleep with one seasick.

Dec 7th

Our watch schedule was split in two hour shifts. With two per watch; 10-12, 12-2, 2-4, 4-6. I took the 10-12, and the 4-6 watches, with a whiskey coke to help.

<img src="/images/blog_posts/image68.jpg" width="100%">

Nairobi had been cloudy and rainy over the past 4 months, and the Stars along the coast keep me awestruck, which was sometime problematic with the fishing boats, some barely presenting any light of their own, I don’t recall any hard turns to avoid them, but the danger was around. We had our glowing ipad map in one corner, the stars above, and a few LED lit boats off in the distance. The new moon only helped the stars shine.

The wind went between gusty to almost dead. At times we changed sailes between light-winders, a lightweight nylon parachute cloth that was oversized, designed for less than 5 kt winds.

Vukulele possessed two forestays and mid-stay. This allowed us to rise and lower a jib without un hanking it. Hanks are frustrating and we had to wash the brass in vinegar and occasionally use a leather-man, but without a furling sail, there’s not much to be done.

We sailed till the sun rose in the East, and headed further out to the North East in hopes to miss the current that would pull us back into Zanzabar and catch a wind on our boardside so we could put up the spinnaker.

While slugging along we caught a pod of dolphins mixed in with Pilot whales. The birds weren’t all that plentiful, somewhat odd.

Around mid day we had made enough head way to put up the spinnaker, the tail wind took us from 2-3 kts up to almost 7kts. Doubling our speed increased confidence, and the doubts if we could even make it back to dar for our flights dissipated. If we continued on our 3-4kts it would have taken us two days to reach Tanga. Luckily we kept on.

The sun set just before we got into the mouth of Tanga; and the wind died with it. We were back to sub 2 knots, and after a few turns behind hills, we were motorless and windless in the mouth of shallow bay, warning buoys scattered about. We had 4 NM to go to the anchorage, it took 5 hours, and left us all hot and miserable.

Brendan had called ahead to secure a dinghy to take us ashore, but at 4am, one wasn’t to be found, and we all ended crashing on the ship hot and sweaty.

Dec 8th

By 7am the sun was too warm to let us sleep and we made our way to the hotel to pass out again.

Brenden, Manual, and I acquired more veggies, ice and beer, then Manual and I joined a walking group to learn about the history of Tanga in the 1st world war.

That night awards were given to 1st and last (us) and everything in between. Much drunkenness was had, along with music from both our contingent and the wedding at the hotel.

<img src="/images/blog_posts/image6.jpg" width="100%">

Dec 9th:

The following morning the race started at 11am, where we started very strong, and then were slowly left behind, possibly saying more about our crews skill than the boat. At the start we kept up with the other boats whom we had the same handicap but we lacked a Genikker which several ships had, and couldn’t keep up.  We also almost mist a required turn. It was frustrating, but spirits we’re up, and we were moving much faster than the previous start.

By nightfall werewer immersed in starts and fishing boats, Their lights blasting the water to attract fish looked like stars on the water. However the wind had died down again, and while we were making 2-3 kts, it didn’t feel like much movement, and steerage was extremely light.

Dec 10th

The next morning we had made 60 NM and had 40NM to go, our point of sail gave us the spinnaker, which we flew until we saw the squall wall coming in behind, and dropped in a hurry.

We had fashioned a sun shade for the protection less helm earlier and put it up for Manual, who despite the heat, was still able to feel the warmth wick away. The storm had given us a push though, and we were straight on towards the Yacht club. By nightfall we were less than 5 NM away and headed well in.

Unfortunately just as were were looking for the finishing line in the dark, we felt the kee drag. We immediately doused sails, and realized we were past the moorings and almost into the Club itself!

We called for assistance (by now it was 10pm), and had two dinghy try and pry us off the sand bed as the whole crew tip the boat to one side trying to get the keel off the bottom. After an hour the boat had gone further in, and it was time to get off.

Brenden sent us on the dinghy, while he stayed with the boat until high tide the next morning.

I escaped to Manuel's guest house, and then was off at 10am on my flight the next morning.

Dec 11th :

Overall we won the most ‘meritorious’ award for our efforts of first race, last both times, and a DNF for our grounding. I received pictures from those still in Dar of the after party, and learned how Brenden had to ensure the bilge was running all night  from water seeping in through windows. Sounded like a rough time.

Aftermath:

After talking to other sailor who had much more experience than us; we learned more about the current vs wind debate. If you know the wind will die, you have to use the current to help pull you along. It’s difficult balance, because many of the currents aren’t mapped as well as the wind, and local sailors are more likely to know what to play than you do ( so if you can keep up and follow them,….)

We had several hiccups in sail changes and managing the different sail configurations which cost us time and distance.

In racing the objective is to move towards the finish in the most efficient way possible, which isn’t always the most direct due to wind, tides, currents, and the physics of turning.

Sailboats move most effectively when they’re at less than a 20˚ angle. When keeling over that 20˚ mark, you’re actually less efficient, and less comfortable. Ergo, if you’re keeled over, you need to reef your sails.

Another point to consider is safety. When deadening down wind, using a spinnaker or a Jenikar are optimal, yet requires more attention. during night watches, when only two are aboard, or when you're overwhelmed this can be difficult.
