June kept moving: the tail of Scotland, then the Solent for two days and nights of examination, then Bristol on foot in a heatwave, then the Cilento coast of Italy for a gathering about food and place. The Yachtmaster Offshore certificate sat at the center of it.

Underneath the travel ran a quieter question, the one I kept circling at the desk between legs: how do you hold the mornings. Not the whole day, just the first hours, before Kate is awake and the messages start, for the projects that actually need making instead of the meta-work that expands to fill any gap. June was the month I noticed how easily I let those hours go.

## Travel

- **Barra, Outer Hebrides**: closing out the Scotland run at Castlebay, the far edge of the country before turning south. A good place to draft the sailing guides I wished I'd had going into the exam.
- **Hamble and the Solent**: the reason for the trip. A week with [Sail Squad](https://www.sailsquad.co.uk/) on the river Hamble, then two days and nights of examination on the Solent, and the RYA Yachtmaster Offshore certificate at the end. The lesson that landed hardest wasn't a tidal calculation: it was the nerve to say "we are going the wrong way" out loud, into the cockpit silence, before the silence turns into a grounding on Bramble Bank. [Full reflections here]({% post_url 2026-06-16-RYA-Yachtmaster %}). Next horizon is Ocean.
- **Bristol**: a day walking the whole city with Kate, staying with friends who've been here since university. An old post-Roman port that grew rich and complicated on the tides, the West-facing harbor, and the slave trade, and has since turned into a crunchy, academic, startup-flavored place with the prestige of London filed off. Best bento bowl in recent memory, a suspension bridge full of students, and a heatwave that had us in sandals hunting shade and water. It broke into a cold front, rain, and a pub, and then a concert by percussionist Dudu Kouaté that was almost mentally exhausting to sit through: he walked on stage, blew into poles in a bucket and looped it, layered shells and drums and flutes made from water bottles, and built the whole thing into something that held tension and then released it.
- **Pollica, Italy**: down the Cilento coast for a gastrodiplomacy gathering, the UNESCO-recognized home of the Mediterranean diet, where [Ancel Keys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancel_Keys) did much of the original research. Dinners with [Sara Roversi](https://www.futurefoodinstitute.org/) and the [Future Food Institute](https://www.futurefoodinstitute.org/) crowd, and long conversations with [Dr. Peter Klosse](https://www.vitalgastronomyinstitute.org/), a former two-star chef who now writes books on taste. His framing stuck with me: most of what we call flavor is mouthfeel, and it reduces to a few moves, volume, sweetness, sour and bitter, and fat, so every dish is a version of those, a metaphor you can then play with.

## Holding the mornings

The solstice is when I redo the [intention calendar]({% post_url 2026-06-17-intention-calendar %}), so the middle of a moving month became the place to stop and take stock. Two things came out of it.

The first was a frustration I finally named: it is too easy to do meta-work with AI, to spend a morning on a broad scan or a giant tooling pass and call it progress, when what the day needed was one specific, shippable thing. The fix isn't more capability, it's a concrete goal per day, small enough to finish before the day gets loud.

The second was the reframe the calendar always forces. This spring I became a Yachtmaster, a free diver, and someone running his own thing. This summer I want to be a tall-ship trip coordinator, a systems consultant, a runner again, and, if I hold myself to it, someone who can strum three songs on a banjo. Naming them is the easy part. The whole point of the wheel is the accountability that comes after.

- [*Consilience*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Consilience+E.O.+Wilson) and the ambition of unifying knowledge across the sciences and humanities. Starting this one; it looks like a big one.
- Bristol as a case study in how a port's wealth, and its harms, still show in the shape of a city centuries later.
- Warm Data and communication as a two-way process, the ideas I want the Island Lab gatherings to be built around.
