We started the month on Barra in the Outer Hebrides and finished in Pollica, southern Italy, with a week of practice for the Yachtmaster Offshore, and more makers, sailors, and musicians in between than I can keep straight!

## Travel

- **Barra, Outer Hebrides**: Kate and I started June on Barra, meeting up with a friend working at a hostel  there and biking the whole island. I was studying for the Yachtmaster while on the ferry over, and took the theory test on the way out of Oban and finished it on the way back. Barra is a complicated place. It reads as Scottish history, but the more we learn about the north the more it looks like a mix of Viking culture and what our archaeologist friend called "French farmers," and the island people and the inland people are different stock. We rented bikes and rode out and back, and I fell into a good conversation with a maker at a bike shop; a French Canadian out of Montreal who is a mechanical engineer and designer who makes bike accessories ([oopsmark.ca](https://www.oopsmark.ca/)). A proper squall came in the day we decided to bike south, and all of us got cozy around the fire. We stayed at the Dunard hostel and were very crammed. Nobody in Europe seems to own a dryer, everyone hang dries, so when you are wet, you are wet. We left a day early.
- **Liverpool**: train from Oban to Glasgow, Glasgow to Liverpool. Stayed with a friend of Kate's, a great window into what a life without kids and with a lot of travel around England and Europe can look like. She is deep into hiking and is sort of a raw foodie, eats cooked food but does not crave it, so it is a lot of apples, granola, yogurt, and cheese, and she is happy eating whatever. She walked us out to the shore at low tide, where the tidal range is enormous, and we picked up the history of the place: Liverpool sat closer to the trades than Scotland or London, heavy in imports and exports and the slave trade, so it has a more industrious feel to what it was tangled up in.
- **Hamble, Yachtmaster Offshore**: down to the Hamble, where we spent Saturday and Sunday on boat parking and studying for the practical. Then one of the more intense weeks of my life: Offshore Yachtmaster, the drills, a lot to talk about. Took the two-day test over the weekend and came out the other side with my Offshore. [Full reflections here]({% post_url 2026-06-16-RYA-Yachtmaster %}).
- **Claygate and the solstice in London**: the following week was in Claygate, with more of Kate's friends, and we had a great time at their place and out to Hampton Court. Then a day in London meeting up with a friend who works at The Economist and others we had met in Scotland, celebrating the solstice. Then back to the Hamble to sail in a completely different register, on a friend's boat this time. They took us down for the day, we slept aboard, met their friends, and I got to cook a barbecue with everybody. A chill, enjoyable week.
- **Bristol**: met up with old project partners from our Kenya days, who now build a learning app ([learn.ink](https://www.learn.ink/)) for workforce development, training, and skills retention across East Africa and parts of sub-Saharan Africa. A big day walking the city, capped by a set from percussionist Dudu Kouaté, who makes music out of everything: started with bubbles, then shells and percussion, capturing the sounds, looping them, and playing it all live. Really good production, all of it on the spot. We stayed an hour and walked home. A good look at what a life in Bristol can be, music and art and a smaller kind of existence.
- **Pollica, Italy**: flight from Bristol to Naples, train down toward Pollica (about two hours south of Naples), then a bus. We have spent the past week as part of a gastrodiplomacy gathering, meeting food producers across the board: fishermen, cheese makers, organic farmers, oil co-ops, and white fig producers (the *fichi bianchi del Cilento*). It is part of the [Future Food Institute](https://www.futurefoodinstitute.org/)'s work in Pollica to publicize what is happening down here, and they have done an incredible job with the mayor on cultural facilitation: celebrating the region's producers and bringing in chefs, policy coordinators, schools, and innovators (which is the bucket we landed in). One more week here before the [Save the Ocean bootcamp](https://climateshapers.org/en/bootcamp-save-the-ocean/).

## Projects

- **Astraeus** ([Astraeus Ocean Systems](https://www.astraeus.eco)): lots of time working on tracking and modeling, and on our work to build coastal monitoring systems. The phase-one "real work" version of this is now pointed at Pollica.
- **Island Lab**: coordinating tall ships and getting people excited about sailing out in the San Juans and the Strait of Juan de Fuca, thinking through what it would take to build living, learning labs out there and pull in artists, craftspeople, and policymakers around the ocean. To get more people onto the water I am experimenting with renting the tall ship *Adventuress* ([Sound Experience](https://www.soundexp.org/)) to learn how she handles and how I can bring people aboard. We're coordinating all of that for our [trip out of Port Townsend here!](https://islandlab.dev/september-2026/salish-voyage.html)

## Reading

- [*Radical Universalism: Beyond Identity*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Radical+Universalism+Omri+Boehm), Omri Boehm (3 stars): a big one to get through. As far as I could tell, the author's project was to argue that a figure could be a racist and still hold philosophical points worth keeping, which read as a laborious thing to defend. The part that landed for me was the throughline on how Martin Luther King and Malcolm X, and for a Christian specifically Jesus, push justice *now* against the "wait" of gradual, structural justice. It was not the most cohesive book. I would point people at some of the conversations inside it rather than recommend the book itself, and it could have been a tighter one-hour talk instead of a short book. Finishing it also clears the way for the Chumstock write-up.
- [*Ra*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Ra+qntm), qntm (recommend): liked the premise, and a huge fan of the conclusion. Magic as a scientific discovery with real bounds, traced through two sisters who studied it at university, one of them later in aerospace, and the question of how power would use it. It turns into a conversation about what distributed, monitored, AI-controlled magic would look like, which goes way left field in a good way. A few threads left hanging, but the writing carried it. The best of the two of theirs I have read, and I would put the prose above Cory Doctorow, who I also enjoy.
- **Robert Jackson Bennett** (on deck): downloaded another of his ahead of an August release. Want to get into one of the Foundryside / Founders books and get back to reading in the mornings.
- [*Healing the Heart of Democracy*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Healing+the+Heart+of+Democracy+Palmer), Parker J. Palmer (still chewing): the same question keeps turning over, how do you demonstrate and protect democratic conciliation, get people involved and believing in their own interactions. Some of that is what I am hoping to do in Pollica, though the fishing piece might sit under "democratic action" as a whole different subset. A topic in itself.
