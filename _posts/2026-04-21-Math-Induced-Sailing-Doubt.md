I'm studying for the RYA Yachtmaster certification, which is a combination of extrapolation math, historic record integration, and decision-making from charts that are themselves records other people have gathered from a local space, put into reference, and then modeled forward so you can think about what the future will look like there. It is a very contorted way of engaging with prediction, your own mental models, and how you relate to a place. And it has been very foreign to me to understand how to integrate it into my existing risk models.

I learned to sail by buying a boat and going out with enough friends to keep me safe. The theory was almost absent. Working through the problems and issues that came up was how we slowly built confidence that we could figure it out rather than stop at the first moment of inertia, locked in problem sets and tests that would have kept us from going out further. The consequences were learned from our actions. Several times I believe we got away with it. We never had a large enough vessel, or enough material in motion, for the consequences to be too high to overcome. Those near-misses led to understanding rather than over-speculation about what was possible.

If I had over-indexed on perfection through theory, I never would have had those experiences. I never would have built the confidence and momentum to go out. I would have kept studying, kept self-doubting, kept asking for more tests to better myself - to learn more, not do more.

While I continue through this theory class, I find it soul-sucking when I know I will step on a boat in the next day or two and completely shift my relationship with the water, the wind, and the race. I will internalize all of that rather than calculate secondary port tide flows or look at the structured way a neap versus a spring tide will move around the corner - all of which were gathered by people who went out, studied, observed, and did, then put it on paper. Which means we are learning from those who did, and then trying to approximate it, rather than doing.

Here's your permission. Go.
