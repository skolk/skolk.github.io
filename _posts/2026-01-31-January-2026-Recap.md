A month of motion. I traveled counterclockwise around the Alps: Zurich, then up to Davos for the World Economic Forum stretch, over to Geneva, into the French Alps at La Grave outside Grenoble, then down into Italy through Bologna, Venice, and finally Barcelona to close out the loop.

I would not generally recommend this much movement to anyone. Two of the stops were for work, several were transit, and the last was a stopover to see a friend. But moving through that many cities in three weeks does sharpen the way you read a place.

## Cities, in the order I saw them

I judge places by their public infrastructure - private infrastructure is harder to step into. When you can choose a restaurant or a museum, you're shaping your experience. Public infrastructure - what you walk through, ride on, can't avoid - is what a place is actually like.

**Zurich** is modern and chic. The trains are on time and efficient. The Swiss use expensive materials that last, with little aesthetic effort beyond function. The towns aren't admirable beyond the fact that they sit in a snowy paradise. There's a warmth they retain while being in an inhospitable place. Davos in winter is similar - functional, warm, but without the finesse or grace I'd find later in France.

**Geneva** still feels Swiss, but with a French grace - and that firm Protestant, Calvinist root. Not Paris with its luxury, not Italy with its gaudiness, but it puts more attention into its ironwork. Small details made for the eye, not exclusively for utility.

**La Grave**, the small ski town outside Grenoble, is named for the seriousness of the area. Steep mountains, almost always in shade. No groomed trails anywhere on the mountain. It brings a certain caliber of skier I've not found anywhere else - and I've never beat up my skis so much on two to three feet of powder light enough that you still scrape rocks the whole way down. The town is maybe fifty stone structures going back to the 1700s, with another similar town further up the hill. The Alps were new to skiing once; the houses are far older. Walking the French Alps, enjoying farmhouses and stone barns, I found much more enjoyable than Davos or Swiss staunchness.

**Bologna** surprised me with its porticoes - arches I found almost nowhere else on the trip. They shelter you from rain (which didn't stop the whole time I was there) and create a geometry to play with, with small cafes underneath them in the morning. The angles of light without mountains changed my perspective on Italy. Where Zurich, Davos, and La Grave all had visible mountains from any vantage point, Bologna built itself up so much that I don't think I saw the horizon the entire time I was there. For the first time on this trip I went on a run. The city let me take pictures and sketch in a way the mountain towns wouldn't. It felt like a medieval Berkeley - test kitchens, wine cellars, restaurants packed with students.

**Venice** is the most confusing man-made structure I've ever been to. Built on islands and slowly sinking, the bridges and alleyways feel like streams. They feel organic in a way Bologna doesn't. Bologna has a center square and everything radiates out from it - clearly man-made. Venice had to bend to the rivers, the islands, the streams - and now to the rising water. I don't think I went more than two minutes without looking at my phone to figure out where I was. Nothing is labeled. Few signs. Easy to get lost everywhere. Boats can take you almost anywhere. Trying to run, walk, or hike around it was extremely confusing and I loved it. It reset what a city could be. I don't think it's a *good* idea. But it was great to see.

**Barcelona**, where I finished, is the easiest of all of them to walk. The widest streets, the most restrictions on cars. A grid, but with many spaces converted to walking corridors. Less stressful than Venice's chaos, less austere than the Alpine towns.

## Looking ahead

Spending more time in Italy is on the list. The European tour of 2026 ends here for now.
