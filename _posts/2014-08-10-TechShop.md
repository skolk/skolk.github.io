## TechShop: ShopBot

I took the cnc class to use the ShopBot. a large wood mill (it can do plastic and foam also) with a gantry of 8' x 4' !!! Which is huge! It's a class I've been trying to get access to for quite a while now- since I've wanted to move into making wooden furniture.

The teachers name was... "Sean." He took us through the basic saftey items and then explained some wooden specific milling concepts. (before this I'd only worked with with metal mills- where we clamped all our material instead of say... having a scratch or sacrificial piece that you bolt down with copper screws and plastic brads.)

After going over operations and making a quick tea light candle holder (it was dumb, all 5 of the students including me threw them away). We reviewed projects that would be worth looking at.

<img src="/images/blog_posts/image56.jpg" width="100%">

Maker Table, which was a version of Atfab's open source furniture is an awesome full length tutorial on how to make a customized table for your hight to fit needs from work bench to computer table. Really excited to make this in the future. In a related note if anyone knows anything about their design process I'd love to hear it!

<img src="/images/blog_posts/image2.png" width="100%">

AtFab! Has a whole list of furniture they will make for you, or you can down load the plans and make yourself. Most of it's quite pricey, so I'll probably just make it on the ShotBot

<img src="/images/blog_posts/image13.jpg" width="100%">

OpenDesk, which AtFab contributes too (it's a small cnc world) allows users to contribute their designs and then a community of over 100 different cnc mills can construct and ship for you.

Open source Bee hives is an example of a project with more than just furniture design in mind. With the end goal of distributing bee hives around the world.

Looking forward to milling. One of the large logistical problems I believe I'll have is transport, so this might be a car centric activity. More on that Later.

Happy Making!

-Sean

## TechShop: Sheet Metal

<img src="/images/blog_posts/image46.jpg" width="100%">

Last night I learned how to make a sheet metal box out of a 16 gauge sheet of steel.

In the process I used:

- Automatic punch
- Hydraulic shear
- Turret punch
- Straight shear
- Corner notcher
- Finger brake
- Hand held riveter

Solid works has a sheet metal tool kit which I'll be experimenting with today, and hopefully post on soon
