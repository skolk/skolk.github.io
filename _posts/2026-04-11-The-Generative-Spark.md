What inspires action? It's easy to consume. In the land of plenty you eat honey. But with standards, with expectations of change, with hardship, or even with a vision of potential, you act differently.

On one end is sloth. On the other is overworked burnout - authoritarianism. So what does leading by inviting look like?

We're looking to invite a few to make, create, and generate. The tension is around what we can come together to make while still allowing others to exist as they are. What is the many-to-many?

This morning I was talking with a few friends about the difference. The idea that identity can help inspire action - or that you can become beholden to it. That is a really important distinction. For some people, identity propels them. For others, it frames their reference in a way that may not be useful. I got immediate pushback from some of the people I was talking to. But the thing we agreed on was the vision you can push, and then the intermediate steps you provide. If you say "let's go sail" but don't offer the in-between actions - rigging, planning, the trip out and back - then you aren't really proposing action. It isn't a responsible invitation. What does it look like to provide the liminal action, and more importantly, the sustained action to keep going?

You frame the context first. The XPRIZE and R2AK do this very well. So do NOLS and many of the mountaineering programs. Here is the large task. Here is the breakdown. Here are the action points. Here are the community and the relationships you'll need.

If I'm talking about community building and many actions, I'd say that putting yourself around people who are both teaching, who have a system, and who are already doing the thing - that is the highest-probability path to success. You become a standard deviation away from the outcome rather than an individual alone looking for systematic change. Tribes and communities are made of people who share common languages and how they think with that language - and then move with each other and take people with them.

This doesn't always mean identifying with the group, though that can help. It means creating common parlance about value sets. If we want to climb a mountain together, we have to understand what climbing means, what hiking means, what a tent is, what camping is, what food we'll need. If we're sailing, we have to know what direction the wind is blowing. We don't have to think of ourselves as sailors, but we have to understand how we are working.

NASA does this extremely well. Not everyone there is an engineer. There are ops people, physicists, astrophysicists, engineers, astronauts, communications staff, nutritionists, doctors, and other scientists - all understanding their slice of a much larger system.

It goes back to stories. Back to transgressing boundaries. Talking to the five-year-old about their potential to be more. Talking to the fifteen-year-old about the work they need to do. Talking to the forty-five-year-old that they're allowed to switch jobs, redefine what success means, change their entire life now that their kids are out of school, drop everything, and move countries. It's about giving permission, pathways, and help.

We start by starting. Then we build the communities, opportunities, and ways for ourselves to keep working with people.
