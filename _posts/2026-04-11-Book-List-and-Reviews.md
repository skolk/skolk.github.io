Going through the list of books I've read in 2025 and 2026, and the ones still sitting on the short and long lists. Several deserve a short review, so here we go.

## 2026 reads

In March 2026 I read [*The Maintenance Race*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=The+Maintenance+Race+Stewart+Brand) by Stewart Brand, which has become one of my favorite pieces of writing. The first chapter, about [Robin Knox-Johnston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robin_Knox-Johnston) and [Donald Crowhurst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Crowhurst), is still one of the best chapters I've read - up there with Rebecca Solnit's first chapter from [*A Paradise Built in Hell*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Paradise+Built+in+Hell+Solnit) on the San Francisco earthquake. Solnit's book is easily in my top ten.

I also read [*Radical Universalism*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Radical+Universalism), a complicated philosophy book that went a lot of ways. The main goal was to identify othering, the mark of Cain, the division of us from them, and MLK's distinction between the technocratic slow people and the radicals of "Justice Now" who we should focus on for action and change. I found it difficult to read, but if the only thing that came out of it was the reminder that we have to pursue the things people need now or they become frustrated and fight, that alone is worth mentioning. I think of this book as a piece in a puzzle, a framework for how to take action and focus on core inclusivity.

I read [*There Is No Antimemetics Division*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=There+Is+No+Antimemetics+Division+qntm) by qntm, which was a wonderful sci-fi short novel. Ideas and counter-ideas and how they fight and think about each other. Enjoyable and quick. Strong recommend. I can't give away too much of what it's about. It was a good sci-fi suspense thriller with a pseudo-horror feel that didn't go fully horror. It was appropriately uncomfortable to move the story forward. Four out of five stars. Strong suggest, not life-changing. I'd like to read more from qntm.

I read [*Circe*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Circe+Madeline+Miller) by Madeline Miller, a reframing of the witch Circe and how she came to be. I really enjoyed the way she frames the Titans and the gods as different - a good reminder of primordial versus egotistical thought. The book mostly focuses on Circe banished to her island, the sailors who come to her, and how she defends herself as an individual while people take from her. I loved the framing of Odysseus from her perspective - as an extremely egotistical hero. A lot of what he did and accomplished wasn't for others. He got most of his crew killed for what ended up being not all that worthy. Even with Athena as his champion, it was almost a psychosis that she enabled in him. Circe respected and enjoyed her time with Telemachus, the son Odysseus didn't raise, who was basically tormented by his father - and with Penelope, the wife. They were victims of his ego. What I love is that they trauma-bond over their time with him and find what to cultivate and work with, not conquer. Circe doesn't need to overcome. She works with. Highly recommend. I'm adding [*The Song of Achilles*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Song+of+Achilles+Madeline+Miller) to the list.

I read [*Brigands and Breadknives*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Brigands+and+Breadknives+Travis+Baldree) by Travis Baldree, the third I've read from him after [*Bookshops & Bonedust*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Bookshops+and+Bonedust+Baldree) and [*Legends & Lattes*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Legends+and+Lattes+Baldree). I enjoyed *Legends & Lattes* - cute, a café in a city. The second one about a bookstore in a small seaside town was kind of an anomaly. They're vaguely queer relationship stories that are light reading, but this one didn't feel like it went anywhere. Mild tension between friends, questionably about a relationship. It doesn't have the fulfilling aspect of [*A Psalm for the Wild-Built*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Psalm+for+the+Wild-Built+Becky+Chambers) or a spiritual book that's exploring an idea. More like a snapshot of life without much challenge to overcome. Okay, but I don't think I'll be reading more of these. Reminded me a little of the Penric novellas by Lois McMaster Bujold.

Before that I read [*Too Like the Lightning*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Too+Like+the+Lightning+Ada+Palmer) by Ada Palmer, which I quite enjoyed. She's extremely intellectual - a historian - and I'm interested in reading more of her work. It's like a Machiavellian Florentine in the future, a Shakespearean frame narrative, extremely wild. Probably too sexual, in versions of a hyper-societal weave. The narrator explains gender to the reader in a way that doesn't feel natural - it feels over-explained. But if you get through that, the rest is silly and fun to explore. I'm enjoying her as a writer, and the second book has been interesting too. High three-point-something, maybe a four.

Before that I read [*The Devils*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=The+Devils+Joe+Abercrombie) by Joe Abercrombie. Kind of a typical D&D quest set in 1300s-1400s Europe. Solid three. Past-the-time character development. It's clear he wants to write a follow-up. Not world-changing.

In February 2026 I read [*The Wonder Engine*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Wonder+Engine+T+Kingfisher), the follow-up to [*Clockwork Boys*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Clockwork+Boys+T+Kingfisher) by T. Kingfisher. Another D&D-esque medium-world-building book. A rogue and a knight end up following love and overcoming their minor quest. Solid three. Okay, wouldn't really recommend it.

Before that I read [*The Raven Scholar*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=The+Raven+Scholar) in January or February 2026. Actually quite decent. I liked the world she built. It follows the progression of someone understanding how they're relating to a competition and accidentally falling into a challenge. 3.5. Better than a lot of others I've read. I'll read the follow-up.

In January 2026 I was reading [Robert Jackson Bennett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Jackson_Bennett) - [*City of Blades*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=City+of+Blades+Robert+Jackson+Bennett), and before that [*City of Stairs*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=City+of+Stairs+Robert+Jackson+Bennett), and his other books in the biopunk era. He's somewhat formulaic in a way that lets him explore the world rather than over-complicate the plot. After his Hugo win, I've enjoyed his thoughts on Instagram. We learned about him at WorldCon 2025, and I'm glad I've dug into his books. I'd highly recommend [*The Tainted Cup*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Tainted+Cup+Robert+Jackson+Bennett) series, and I'll be reading more of those.

## Earlier reads

I read [*The Invisible Doctrine*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Invisible+Doctrine+Monbiot) by George Monbiot, on the history of neoliberalism and how it emerged from various power-hungry factions. It touches on Adam Smith and what Smith said versus how it's been extrapolated, but mostly stays in the 20th century, identifying how capitalism emerged and how powerful people need ways for capital to find itself and justify what it's doing for industry and power - under the guise of utility for everyone else. A great reflection on the words versus actions of a lot of powerful groups, and how you can appeal to the intellectualism of utility and then do the complete opposite and just capture money for your own ends. It's one of those books that shows the undercurrent versus the overcurrent: a median narrative versus what rent-seeking elites are actually doing, and how the economic intellectuals have become their own justification echo chamber. The populist parts that emerge are different from what is actually going on technocratically. An okay book. There are probably a few of his speeches I'd recommend instead. Glad I read it.

[*Things Become Other Things*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Things+Become+Other+Things+Craig+Mod). Living in Bellingham, I was introduced to [Craig Mod](https://craigmod.com/) by a series of pushes he made. I enjoy his story quite a lot - orphan-turned-hacker-modifier-writer who lived in Japan as a way to explore his creative self. He also organizes walks that I thoroughly enjoy the design of, bringing people together to coordinate experiences. *Things Become Other Things* is him growing up and exploring his relationship with a friend who was an anchoring point in his earlier life. I hold Craig as a staple, an idea of a person I could become or learn from. I'll definitely follow more of his work.

Before that I read [*The Book of Elsewhere*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Book+of+Elsewhere+Keanu+Reeves+Mieville) by Keanu Reeves and China Miéville. One of the more bizarre fantasy books I've read. Miéville is a wonderful writer and I enjoy a lot of what he's done. A good wandering into a different world. Solid three. An interesting way to spend time with the concept of immortality - what 30,000 or 40,000 years of life might do to relationships, and how they could become dull to the point of questioning meaning.

I read [*We Are Legion (We Are Bob)*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=We+Are+Legion+We+Are+Bob+Dennis+Taylor) by Dennis E. Taylor, a short read on brain uploading and becoming a spaceship and a multitude in the future. Quite a lot of fun. Silly. Very quick. 3.5.

Before that I read a series of [Emily Tesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emily_Tesh) books. [*Some Desperate Glory*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Some+Desperate_Glory+Emily+Tesh) is worth reading. Solid four. I believe it won the Hugo or Nebula. It's about the radicalization of radicalized people, and what it means to think about authoritarianism or fascism as a one-to-many control mechanism versus individual relationships, and how you work with those people in groups. Pretty high up there. Emily's process of doing extensive research on topics and then writing books is something I aspire to. Maybe I should write more short stories. Maybe everything I'm reading should better coalesce into that structure.

In June or July 2025, a friend challenged me to read more romantasy to understand a female perspective on certain books. I read [*A Court of Thorns and Roses*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=A+Court+of+Thorns+and+Roses+Maas), which I found completely ridiculous but comically entertaining in the same way *Twilight* was. I followed up with [*Kushiel's Dart*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Kushiel's+Dart+Jacqueline+Carey), much better written, with the same undertone of how do we relate to a person and then sleep with them. I actually enjoyed it and read one of the follow-ups. This whole genre is a good reminder that everyone is looking for different things in a story.

Before this I had a series of books by [Lois McMaster Bujold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lois_McMaster_Bujold) - [*Penric and the Demon*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Penric+and+the+Demon+Bujold), and [*The Curse of Chalion*](https://www.goodreads.com/search?q=Curse+of+Chalion+Bujold). The realm of the five gods, mostly focusing on the Bastard, but also the Son, the Daughter, the Mother, and the Father. I enjoy female writers who don't focus on one specific entity-and-enemy structure - it seems like a great perspective to work through and with.
