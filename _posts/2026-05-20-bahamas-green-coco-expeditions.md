We were barely off the dock when the dolphins showed up. The dinghy from *Nesi* had just collected us, and there they were in the bow wave, so everyone went over the side. The water was warm, the sun was out, the exact opposite of the cold we had flown out of. That was the first hour, and it set the tone for the week.

The Bahamas keeps its life underwater. The land is low and rocky and baked, in what looks like a long drought of ebb and flow. The whole archipelago is a shelf of calcium carbonate, millions of years of shellfish and maybe coral, and above the waterline it reads like desert. Drop below it and the place turns over. That contrast was the trip.

This was a free-diving week, coordinated by Audrey, a long-time sailing partner and a free-diving instructor who does a lot of Reef Check work around San Francisco and Monterey. We met up with [Green Coco Expeditions](https://greencoco.org/), partway through an around-the-world voyage on *Nesi*, their 60-foot catamaran. Kristian and Sabrina run it, with their two daughters aboard and Jana along as well. There were about ten of us.

The best part of someone else's boat is everything you don't have to do. No provisioning day, no check-in or check-out, that is what you are paying for and it is worth it. Kate and I had our own bunk. They ran Starlink, so a little work crept in around the edges. Mostly the days held a simple shape: wake up, swim, breakfast, move the boat or go diving.

Audrey ran the diving like a course. We had homework, static breath holds, depth holds, talks and breathing sessions. Most mornings opened with yoga and stretching before breakfast. Then we would swim out over the shelf and work down past ten meters, a little deeper as the week went on.

We anchored away from town to feel what it was like to be out there, and we worked our way through the remote cays, Stone Cay, Water Cay, down toward Flamingo Key, where the flamingos are gone but the water is thick with shrimp. We took *Nesi* through the Hog Cut in less than a meter of water, which is its own kind of stressful in a 60-foot catamaran. This time of year the Bahamas gets frequent storms, and we watched squalls build and pass, hard and fast and then gone. Remote, but with a lot of people, which is its own thing to manage. A lot of the week was spent staring at horizons, watching weather come and go.

The diving was the payoff for the thesis. Where the shelf drops off, everything changes. The cold deep water lets a different plant life hold on, and the fish stack up against it. We found a few blue holes, and at one of them a whole school of jacks turned through, then turtles. On land it was a baking desert. Below, it was swells of life. I understand now why people want to be down there.

Best charter I've been on. I want to bring that energy to our own Boatless Sailing trips. Not sure when we'll be back, but I would go again, and I'm happy to share the charts for the area. Green Coco Expeditions, highly recommend.
