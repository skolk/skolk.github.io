# Race to Alaska (R2AK)

<!-- Photo slot: drop a file at /images/projects/r2ak.jpg, then delete the comment markers on the next line. -->
<!-- <img src="/images/projects/r2ak.jpg" width="100%" alt="Team Natural Disaster racing the Olson 30 in R2AK"> -->

The Race to Alaska is 750 miles from Port Townsend to Ketchikan with no engine, no support, and one rule that matters: if you break it, you fix it yourself or you are done. In 2024 I skippered [Team Natural Disaster](https://r2ak.com/2024-teams-full-race/team-natural-disaster/) on an Olson 30 and finished the Inside Passage in 7 days, 5 hours, 14 minutes. This page is the project frame: the boat, the human propulsion, the route, and the crew model. The blow-by-blow lives in the [2024 trip report]({% post_url 2024-06-20-r2ak2024 %}).

## The problem

An engineless ocean race rewards preparation over speed. You are solving for self-reliance: a boat that can sail in everything from a flat calm to 25 knots on the nose, a way to move when the wind dies, the spares and skills to repair a failure underway, and a crew rotation that keeps people functional on no sleep for a week. The race is less a sprint than a rolling test of every system, including the people.

## The boat

An Olson 30: light, quick downwind, and able to bash through rough seas, the kind of boat that flies under a symmetrical spinnaker and still claws to weather when it has to. Six hours in, the rod rigging failed at the masthead and the headstay let go. We jury-rigged spinnaker halyards to the bow deck plate as temporary forestays, coordinated a replacement up from Sidney, docked under sail and pedal power beside a rigging tower, and were back racing within hours. The boat that finishes R2AK is the boat you can fix.

## Human propulsion

No engine means a pedal drive and oars. When the wind died in Johnstone Strait and the tide gates, even one knot of pedal power beat sitting at anchor: two people pedaling against current, switching out every 20 to 40 minutes, making 1.8 to 2 knots and holding position in places with no anchorage. Reading current edges and eddies to find favorable flow mattered as much as sail trim.

## The route

Port Townsend north through the Strait of Georgia, the Seymour Narrows and Johnstone Strait tide gates, out around Cape Caution into Queen Charlotte Sound, the outside passage around Calvert Island through Hakai to the Bella Bella checkpoint, then the Pacific route up past Banks Island to Dixon Entrance and the finish at Ketchikan. The tide gates are where seamanship has to actually be seamanship: 7 knots of adverse current at Seymour Narrows turns racing into timing.

## The crew model

Watches of four hours on and four off, with a rolling two hours on call between, staggered so someone was always fresh. The hard part of R2AK is rarely the sailing; it is functioning, deciding, and repairing on accumulated sleep deprivation. The crew that finishes is the one that keeps working together when everything that can go wrong does. R2AK gave Kate and me just enough structure to find a vehicle for achievement and a community to help us along the way.
