# Island Lab

> Island Lab is Kate Schnippering and Sean Kolk: sailors, designers, engineers, and gatherers of problem-solvers who bring people together in and around the water to better understand how we all coexist with it. The vessel and the islands are the venue. The cohort is the program.

This is the human-scale project among the four. It is not software, although the design discipline is the same. It is a format: small group, tight constraints, many-to-many generative structure, hosts as vehicle providers rather than centers of gravity. The boat enforces the constraints we would otherwise have to manufacture.

## The Problem

The corporate offsite market is shifting toward purposeful, place-based, low-screen formats. The retreat-center genre is saturated and increasingly indistinguishable. The convening genre (climate weeks, summits, conferences) is sized to network, not to think.

The format I want sits between those. Small enough that everyone is in every conversation. Constrained enough that the work has to actually finish in the room. Tied to a place that is doing something interesting (the Salish Sea is doing several things). And honest about what the format costs and what it can't do.

The piece that's actually hard is not the boat. The boat is a working tall ship that already exists. The hard piece is the program design that turns four days under sail into a generative artifact instead of a sightseeing charter.

## What It Produces

Per voyage, three things. A cohort that has done shared work under shared constraint, which is the durable output. An artifact (a written piece, a framework, a prototype, a sharper question) that did not exist before the week began. And a small set of warm relationships that, if the voyage is partnered with an institution like Sound Experience, feed that institution's longer arc.

Beyond the voyage itself, a playbook. What worked, what changed, pricing structure for the next cohort, the cohort-selection rubric that produced this group, the pre-read packet, the post-voyage synthesis materials. Each voyage feeds the next.

## How It Works

The unit is a three- or four-day overnight aboard a working vessel. Cohort sizes between eight and twenty. Composition mixed: corporate sponsors, technical or domain practitioners, and a small wildcard component (one or two "creative friction" participants who would not be in the room otherwise).

The daily rhythm is unromantic. Morning watch under sail with participants on crew rotation. Midday programming at anchor, structured generative work in the Chumstock model (many-to-many, participant-driven, hosts providing scaffolding rather than substance). Afternoon ashore, a field component tied to a relevant site. Evening as program, dinner with structured reflection and informal pairing.

The operational shape is two entities. Island Lab (the LLC) contracts with participants and runs the program. The vessel operator (Sound Experience for the September 2026 pilot, possibly other partners later) charters the boat to Island Lab. Both entities hold their own insurance. Tuition flows into Island Lab, charter fees flow out to the vessel operator, both clear without taking on each other's risk.

## Worked Example: the September 2026 Sound Experience pilot

This is the first one. It is happening as I write this.

### What we ran

Adventuress, four days, Thursday September 17 through Sunday September 20, departing Port Townsend, working through the Strait of Juan de Fuca into the lower San Juans and back. The partnership is with Sound Experience, a nonprofit that runs Adventuress as a working educational tall ship. Their shoulder season is our window.

The cohort target is fifteen to twenty participants. Three pricing tiers, all live on the public voyage page: Supporter at $2,500 for those whose means or institution can give more, Professional at $1,600, and a small Artist/early-career allocation at $1,000. The deep-discount tier from earlier pricing iterations was retired in May.

Programming theme is still in selection between three candidates that came out of conversations with Sound Experience's science team: coastal monitoring network design, blue-economy workforce pathways, and climate-resilient food systems in the Salish Sea. The theme decision gates the Julie call, co-host commitment, and cohort recruiting.

### What it produced

So far, the operational scaffolding. Vessel dates confirmed. Pricing live on the public page. Cohort rubric drafted. Insurance brief drafted. Co-lead brief drafted. LLC formation in process. A two-tier dinner at Shilshole on July 19 confirmed as the soft introduction event, no cost to Sound Experience.

The voyage itself runs in September. Cohort selection deadline is August 1. Go/no-go decision is August 15, conditional on fourteen confirmed seats with at least ten in the Professional tier.

### What surprised us

The seam between two entities running two halves of the same engagement is more interesting than I expected. The operating model (Island Lab runs program, Sound Experience runs vessel, both hold their own insurance, charter fee flows clean) is the thing that makes this repeatable. If the pilot proves it works, the same shape ports to other working vessels in the Pacific Northwest without rebuilding the relationship from scratch.

The other surprise is how much voice matters. The site has been flagged for sounding AI-written in places, and that's the bug. Island Lab's voice (literary but plain, warm without sentimental, specific over generic, honest about constraints) is part of the product. A "transformative immersive experience" pitch loses the audience this format is for. "Narrow bunks, dry ship, vegetarian galley, four reasonably demanding days" finds them.

### What it costs, what scales

The fixed cost base for one voyage is approximately $46,000, including vessel charter ($24,000), program design and facilitation, co-lead honorarium, insurance, materials, and a 10% contingency. At twenty seats, that is about $2,380 per seat to break even. The three-tier pricing requires a Supporter-heavy cohort or external sponsorship to clear, which is a deliberate design choice (lower the floor, raise the ceiling, accept a tighter selection target).

The format scales to maybe three voyages a year in the Pacific Northwest before it needs a second host pair. Past that, the model is to write the playbook and let other host pairs run their own cohorts in their own places, not to scale the original.

## Where It's Going

September pilot runs. Playbook gets written by end of October. 2027 calendar gets sketched: two to three voyages, themes drawn from what the September cohort surfaces. The longer fork (after 2027) is the open decision between "Buy Salt Spring" (a small land base in the Pacific Northwest) and "Scale the Ship" (more voyages on more partner vessels). Both are on the table.

## Status

Seeking partners and seeking the first cohort. The voyage is sized for fifteen to twenty participants by August 1. I'd like to hear from: people who do work at the intersection of coastal systems, blue economy, and community design; institutions thinking about how to run their own small generative formats; and the kind of participant who finds the "narrow bunks and four demanding days" framing more attractive than the "transformative experience" framing.
