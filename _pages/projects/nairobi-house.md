# Nairobi Communal House

A year-long group housing experiment. Kate and I got a large house in Nairobi and hosted between five and ten people throughout the course of the year. The point was to test, in real conditions, what facilitated group living actually requires.

## The setup

A house big enough to hold the experiment. A rotating cast of guests and residents (five to ten people at any given time), some for weeks, some for months, some passing through. Kate and I were the hosts and the curators: deciding who came, holding the rhythm of the house, and quietly doing the work that keeps a shared space from devolving into a shared inconvenience.

## What we were testing

Whether a house, run with intention, can be a better unit of life than the apartment-plus-friendships pattern most people default to. What "facilitated" actually has to mean in practice (more than vibes, less than a constitution). What it costs the hosts. What it gives back. Where the failure modes are and which of them are worth designing around.

## What I took away

A working theory of how much structure a group house needs (less than people think, more than zero) and a strong sense that the host role is the load-bearing variable. Most communal living failures are not about the people; they are about the absence of someone actively holding the shape.

## Status

Concluded after a year. Lessons still in active use.
